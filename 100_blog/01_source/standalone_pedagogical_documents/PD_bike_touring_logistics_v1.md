# Front Matter
**Title:** Bicycle Touring Logistical Baselines
**Target Audience:** 15-year-old ESL reader (CEFR B2+)
**Governing MoS:** Pedagogical Document Style (Enforces CEFR B2+ accessibility register, 6,500 common-word vocabulary ceiling, and concrete-before-abstract scaffolding)
**Prerequisite Chapters:** None
**Estimated Reading Time:** 15 minutes

## Chapter 1: How Far to Ride Each Day

On the first day of a long trip, many new riders plan to cover eighty kilometers. After twenty kilometers, their legs already feel heavy and the road ahead looks longer than it did on the map. This happens because the body has not yet adjusted to carrying a loaded bicycle for many hours in a row.

For younger or newer riders, a safe distance for the first week is thirty to fifty kilometers a day. After the first week, sixty to eighty kilometers a day becomes possible for most riders in good health.

For older or retired riders, thirty to fifty kilometers a day works well as a steady daily distance. Heat and hills matter more than fitness level when deciding how far to ride.

For every rider, the planned distance for early days should be treated as the most a person will ride, not the goal. Riding less than planned on early days helps the body recover. Riding more than planned on early days often leads to injury later.

## Chapter 2: When to Rest

A rider who stops for ten minutes every hour, even when feeling strong, arrives at the end of the day with less pain in the knees and back than a rider who pushes through without stopping.

Every rider should take a short break of about ten minutes every hour, on and off the bike.

Older riders should plan one full day of rest for every three or four days of riding. Younger riders can often wait five to seven days between full rest days, or stop sooner if they feel joint pain, sore skin from the saddle, or ongoing tiredness.

On trips longer than one month, every rider should take one full day per week with no riding at all, not even short errands on foot.

## Chapter 3: What to Sleep In

A rider caught in a sudden rainstorm learns quickly whether their tent keeps water out at the seams and whether their sleeping bag stays warm when the air turns cold at night.

For shelter, a two person tent that weighs less than two kilograms works well for most trips. Riders who travel alone and want to carry less weight can use a simple bivy sack together with a small tarp instead of a tent.

For warmth, a sleeping bag or quilt should be rated for a temperature five degrees Celsius colder than the coldest night expected on the trip, not the average night.

For the ground, a sleeping pad should match the season and location. A thinner pad works for warm weather. A thicker pad is needed for cold nights or high mountain areas. Older riders should choose a thicker pad even if it weighs more, because comfort for the hips and back matters more than saving weight.

## Chapter 4: Staying Healthy on the Road

A rider who breaks an ankle two days from the nearest town learns the hard way why health planning matters before the trip begins, not after an injury happens.

Every rider crossing international borders must carry travel health insurance that includes medical evacuation. Medical evacuation means a company will pay to fly a badly injured or sick rider to a hospital that can treat them properly. Many standard travel insurance policies do not cover bicycles or evacuation from remote areas, so this must be checked and confirmed before the trip.

Every rider who takes regular medication should carry a letter from a doctor. The letter should list each medicine by its general chemical name, not its brand name, because brand names change from country to country. The letter should be written in English and, when possible, in the language of the countries the rider will pass through.

Every rider should carry a basic first aid kit. This kit should include antiseptic for cleaning cuts, treatment for blisters, oral rehydration salts for treating dehydration, strips for closing small wounds, and any personal medical items such as an inhaler or allergy injector.

Older riders should carry some extra items beyond this basic kit. This includes a copy of recent heart test results, called an electrocardiogram, if the rider has any heart condition. It also includes at least two extra weeks of any regular medication, in case of travel delays. Finally, older riders should decide in advance what they will do if a small injury takes a full week to heal instead of a single day. This usually means knowing ahead of time about a low cost place to stay near the route, instead of assuming they can simply keep camping.

## Chapter 5: Tools That Do More Than One Job

A rider whose only cooking pot cracks in the middle of a remote stretch learns to use the same pot as a mixing bowl and a plate, because carrying a separate item for each task is not possible.

In the cooking kit, one pot can serve as a bowl and a mixing container. One spoon shaped tool, called a spork, can replace a fork, spoon, and knife. The same stove used to boil water for cooking can also boil water to make it safe to drink.

In the repair kit, a tire lever can also work as a flathead screwdriver. Plastic zip ties can fix both mechanical problems and torn gear. A short length of strong tape wrapped around a water bottle can replace a full roll of tape.

In the hygiene kit, one cotton cloth, called a bandana, can protect the neck from sun, filter dust from the air, and serve as a small towel. One bar of biodegradable soap can wash the body, dishes, and light laundry.

## Chapter 6: Traveling Alone or With Another Person

A rider traveling alone through a stretch with no phone signal for two days learns why a simple mobile phone is not enough safety equipment for remote travel.

A rider traveling alone should carry a satellite communicator, which is a small device that can send messages and an emergency signal even where there is no phone network. This rider should also agree with one fixed contact at home on how often to check in, such as every day or every two days.

Two riders traveling together should agree before the trip on a simple rule. Either rider can call for a full rest day or a change of route without needing to explain the reason. Two riders should also each carry their own complete small set of repair tools and shelter items, so that if one person's bag is lost, the other person is not left without basic supplies.

## Glossary

**Medical evacuation**: a service that flies a badly injured or sick person to a hospital able to treat them, usually included in travel insurance.

**Electrocardiogram**: a medical test that records the electrical activity of the heart.

**Bivy sack**: a small waterproof cover for a sleeping bag, used instead of a full tent.

**R-value**: a number that shows how well a sleeping pad blocks cold from the ground. A higher number means more warmth.

**Satellite communicator**: a small handheld device that sends messages and emergency signals using satellites instead of phone networks.
