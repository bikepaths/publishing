In 2026, America faces an alarming reality. The richest country in history grapples with a crisis that casts a long shadow over its streets—homelessness. Despite having the wealth and resources to house everyone, the current system remains ineffective. Take the story of a woman sitting against a concrete wall in Los Angeles. Her eyes do not track the world around her; she exists in an internal state where decision-making feels impossible. An outreach worker approaches her, but instead of offering help, he logs her as “service declined.” What the record won’t show is the complex interaction of her nervous system, reacting to years of danger and instability. This experience is not unique; it reflects the broader struggle of 745,000 individuals facing homelessness every night across the U.S. 

The key to understanding this paradox lies in the human brain’s response to trauma and stress. When sitting in a state of fight-or-flight or complete shutdown, a person’s ability to engage with the idea of housing becomes virtually negligible. Cognitive functions take a backseat while survival instincts take over. This means that the standard offer for housing fails miserably because it is handed to someone whose brain can’t process it. It’s like asking someone running from a bear if they’d like to sit down for tea; the question simply cannot be heard or understood. Yet, our systems continue to treat these situations as refusals, closing cases and redirecting resources away from those who need help the most. This misunderstanding runs deep, costing taxpayers an average of fifty thousand dollars a year for each unsheltered person, yet resulting in no change for these individuals’ circumstances.

The challenges are not just logistical; they are steeped in a political landscape that systematically neglects the very people it aims to assist. Individuals experiencing homelessness often have little political voice. They lack the visibility that motivates action among those who can make changes. The system around housing has transformed. It’s no longer just a basic need but a financial asset, making it easier for property owners to profit off scarcity than to address the immediate needs of the unhoused. Moreover, the social networks that form within encampments provide crucial support, yet navigating the transition into housing typically severs those connections. This leaves the individual feeling more isolated, much like being thrown into a pool without a life preserver—it’s overwhelming and frightening.

As we confront the simple truth that we have options and insight that have not been utilized, we see that the path forward doesn’t require revolutionary new ideas but the application of existing knowledge. A model called Material Dignity Infrastructure suggests that if outreach is done in a manner that respects the biological state of a person, the offer of housing becomes more accessible. It emphasizes patience, understanding, and the human element in providing services. The roots of our social infrastructure are already established, but they need to be cultivated in a way that brings about understanding and awareness. When that effort aligns, we can transform not only the streets but human lives trapped in a cycle of survival—a cycle that could be broken, one compassionate connection at a time.
