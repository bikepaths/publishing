The Paradox of the Mirror and the Elephant
Imagine walking into a room filled with billions of people, and every single person is holding up a mirror.
If you ask the first person what God is like, they point at their mirror and say, "God is a fierce warrior, just like me." The next person points to theirs and says, "No, God is a quiet artist, just like me." A third says God is a strict judge, a fourth says a gentle healer, and a fifth says God is a mysterious force in the trees. Every person looks into their own glass, sees their own reflection, and calls it the Divine.
This brings us to a massive, mind-twisting puzzle.
If we just change God to match our own faces, our own rules, and our own favorite ideas, we aren’t actually looking for a Creator. We are just looking in a mirror. And if there are eight billion people on Earth, and everyone invents their own personal version of God, then the word "God" stops meaning anything at all. If a thousand people look at a cloud and see a thousand different shapes—a dragon, a castle, a shoe—the cloud isn't actually any of those things. It's just a cloud. If God is only whatever we want God to be, then maybe there is no God at all.
But there is another way to look at the mystery.
Think about a group of blindfolded scientists who are led into a room to examine a strange creature they have never encountered before. The first scientist holds the creature’s swinging trunk and exclaims, "Aha! This beast is exactly like a giant, slithering snake!" The second scientist, hugging one of the creature's massive legs, argues, "Don't be ridiculous! This creature is like a solid, rough tree trunk!" A third scientist strokes the flat side of the animal and shouts, "You are both wrong! It is a flat, hairy wall!"
The scientists begin to shout and argue, each insisting their own definition is the absolute truth. Because their definitions are so completely different, a bystander watching them might walk away thinking, These people are just making things up. There is no real animal in that room.
But the bystander would be wrong.
The elephant is completely real, completely whole, and completely unchanged by the scientists' confusion. The problem isn't that the elephant doesn't exist; the problem is that the scientists are limited. They can only feel one small piece of the truth at a time. Their conflicting definitions don't erase the elephant; they just prove how big the elephant really is.
This is the grand paradox. If a True God exists, that God must be the exact same independent reality for everyone, everywhere—whether humans understand it or not. A real God cannot be a mirror that changes shapes depending on who is looking.
So the arrow of life has to point the other way. We cannot bend the ultimate truth of the universe to look like us. Instead, like those scientists taking off their blindfolds, or like an apprentice builder studying a master blueprint, our job is to grow our minds, expand our hearts, and climb higher. We don't try to make God look like us; we try to grow wise enough to see the whole elephant.
