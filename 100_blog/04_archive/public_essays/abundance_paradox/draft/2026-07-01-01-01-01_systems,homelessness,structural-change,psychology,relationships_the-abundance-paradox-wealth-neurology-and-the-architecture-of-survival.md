### tl;dr

Because chronic street exposure triggers dorsal-vagal shutdown that renders conventional housing offers biologically unreceivable, resolving urban encampments requires managed care financing to fund relational architecture that matches autonomic states while preserving verified survival networks indoors.

#### Reading time averages fifteen minutes. 

Word count totals 3136 words. 
Long-form narrative structure.

She had been sitting against the retaining wall on East Sixth Street for what a passing pedestrian might have called hours, though time had stopped meaning much to her nervous system long before noon. Her posture had collapsed inward, chin resting on her sternum, hands open and motionless on the concrete beside her thighs. Her breathing was so shallow that the outreach worker who approached her had to watch for nearly thirty seconds before confirming she was alive. He crouched six feet away, said her name, waited. Her eyes were open. They did not track his voice. He placed a bottle of water beside her left hand, noted the interaction on his tablet, checked a box labeled "Service Declined," and moved to the next person on his route.

That box would follow her through three county databases. It would appear on her file when the next worker pulled her name. It would inform the next offer, which would arrive with slightly less institutional patience than this one, because the system had now recorded evidence that she did not want housing. A caseworker reviewing the file in six months would see a person who had been offered help and refused it. The caseworker would not see what the outreach worker himself had not been trained to recognize, which was that the woman on the sidewalk had not refused anything. Her prefrontal cortex, the part of the brain that evaluates choices, imagines a future, and weighs one option against another, was not functioning at the moment the offer was made. Chronic danger had shut it down. Her nervous system had dropped into a conservation state that suspends voluntary cognition to preserve metabolic resources, a state as involuntary as a flinch. The offer was delivered to a body in biological emergency. It was not received. Recording the non-response as a decision was a factual error that would now compound across every future interaction.

She would cost the public approximately fifty thousand dollars that year. Emergency department visits when someone finally called 911 after she stopped moving for too long. Psychiatric holds triggered by welfare checks. Law enforcement contacts for trespassing on the property where she sheltered. Sanitation crews dispatched to clean the site after she moved or was moved. None of these expenditures would produce a durable change in her condition. None of them were designed to. The fifty thousand dollars was the annual cost of managing a person on the street, and it would recur next year, and the year after that, until she died or until something structurally different happened.

This is a story about why that structurally different thing has not happened. It is a story about a civilization with more wealth, more technology, and more accumulated knowledge about this specific problem than any civilization in recorded history, and about the forces that prevent all of that capacity from reaching the people who need it most. It is also a story about what the evidence says would work, and about the distance between knowing what would work and doing it.

The common-sense position on street homelessness is disarmingly simple. Either people want housing or they do not. If they do, the system should house them. If they do not, the system should focus its limited resources on the people who are ready. This logic seems self-evident. It also contains an assumption about what wanting means that collapses under scrutiny.

Wanting, in the sense that matters for a housing offer, is not a feeling. It is a neurological capacity. To want housing in a way that produces a yes, a person must be able to hold two states of the world in mind simultaneously: the state they are in now and the state the offer describes. They must compare those states, imagine inhabiting the second one, and make a judgment. This comparison requires the prefrontal cortex, and the prefrontal cortex requires a nervous system that is not in emergency mode. The difficulty is that chronic street exposure pushes the nervous system into exactly the emergency mode that disables this capacity.

Polyvagal theory, the framework that clinical trauma research has used for three decades to map the body's autonomic responses to danger, identifies three distinct states. In the first state, called ventral vagal engagement, the body is calm enough for social connection. Breathing is deep. Eye contact is possible. Language carries meaning. A person in this state can hear an offer, weigh it, and respond. In the second state, sympathetic arousal, the body has flooded with adrenaline. The person paces, scans for threats, speaks rapidly or not at all. Deliberation is impossible because the body is preparing to fight or run. In the third state, dorsal vagal shutdown, the nervous system has determined that the threat is inescapable and has responded by shutting down voluntary activity to conserve metabolic energy. Breathing becomes shallow. The body goes still. The face goes flat. Cognition narrows to the immediate moment, and the social engagement system that processes language, evaluates offers, and imagines futures goes offline.

The woman on East Sixth Street was in the third state. The outreach worker delivered a first-state offer to a third-state nervous system. The offer could not land. This is not a metaphor. It is a description of what happens when the part of the brain that processes decisions is suspended by the part of the brain that manages survival. What the system recorded as a refusal was a biological event that the individual did not generate, could not control, and should not be held accountable for.

The implication is unsettling and precise. Many of the people whom the homelessness system has classified as refusing housing have never been offered housing under conditions where their nervous systems could receive the offer. The system is not failing to persuade them. It is failing to reach them. The refusal is an artifact of the system's inability to recognize what state a person is in before making the approach. A trained worker who understood autonomic states would have looked at the woman on East Sixth Street, recognized the collapsed posture and the shallow breathing and the vacant gaze, and would not have made the offer at all. That worker would have sat nearby for two minutes without speaking, left the water, noted the biological state in the contact log, and returned Thursday. The offer would wait. It would wait because trust accumulates on a biological timeline that institutional calendars cannot compress, and because pushing the offer through a shutdown nervous system does not speed the process. It damages it.

The chronically unsheltered are not a population the system ignores. They are a population the system manages, continuously and at extraordinary expense.

Los Angeles County spends roughly fifty thousand dollars per person per year on individuals living on the street. The figure aggregates emergency department admissions at rates far exceeding the housed population, psychiatric holds under the Lanterman-Petris-Short Act that cycle individuals through seventy-two-hour evaluations and release them to the same sidewalk, law enforcement contacts that generate booking costs without producing behavioral change, and sanitation operations that clear encampment sites only to see them reconstitute within weeks. A 2024 HUD Exchange evidence brief documented that permanent supportive housing targeted to heavy service users generated gross savings exceeding forty-six thousand dollars per person per year compared to the cost of street management. The savings came not from the housing itself but from the elimination of the emergency services the housed individual no longer required.

California spent twenty-four billion dollars on homelessness programs between 2019 and 2024. During the same five-year window, the unsheltered population grew to over one hundred and eighty-seven thousand individuals. The California State Auditor confirmed these figures in a 2024 report that traced the expenditure and measured the outcome. The numbers do not describe a resource shortage. They describe a system that is spending vast sums and producing the opposite of its stated objective.

The triage objection arises naturally from these numbers. If the system cannot house the cooperative population, why invest its most complex resources in the population that appears to resist engagement? The answer is structural. The fiscal architecture proposed by the MDI working paper series routes housing for the chronically unsheltered through managed care billing under California's CalAIM waiver, specifically through Enhanced Care Management and In Lieu of Services pathways that convert relational outreach into reimbursable clinical infrastructure. These billing mechanisms operate under existing managed care contracts. They do not compete with the general fund appropriations that finance rapid rehousing, transitional shelters, and emergency vouchers for the broader homeless population. The model adds capacity. It does not redirect existing capacity. This is a design claim, and it requires empirical testing at the prototype stage, but the objection that housing the hardest cases diverts resources from the easiest ones misreads the funding structure.

The year is 2026. The gross domestic product of the United States exceeds twenty-eight trillion dollars. California alone generates an economy larger than all but four nations. The technological infrastructure to design, build, and manage permanent supportive housing exists and has existed for decades. The clinical knowledge base required to understand autonomic states and their effect on engagement has been accumulating since Stephen Porges published the polyvagal framework in 1994. The fiscal data establishing that street management costs more than housing has been available since Dennis Culhane's landmark 2002 study documented that Housing First participants generated over sixteen thousand dollars per person per year in avoided public costs. Every component of a working solution is present. The solution has not been assembled.

Four structural forces explain the gap, and each operates independently of the others, which is why addressing any single force leaves the problem intact.

The first force is political voice asymmetry. The chronically unsheltered do not vote in proportion to their numbers. They do not fund campaigns. They do not staff lobby organizations. They do not attend public hearings because attending a public hearing requires a stable address, transportation, cognitive availability, and the belief that one's presence will matter. The organized interests that benefit from the current system are present at every hearing, every budget cycle, and every procurement decision. Mancur Olson described this dynamic in 1965 when he demonstrated that concentrated interests with organizational capacity act against dispersed beneficiaries who cannot coordinate in proportion to their aggregate welfare stake. The aggregate benefit of housing the chronically unsheltered is large. The per-person incentive to organize for that benefit is vanishingly small. The per-organization incentive to protect existing contracts is enormous.

The second force is the financialization of housing. Property values in Los Angeles, San Francisco, New York, and Seattle are not incidental to the homelessness crisis. They are causal. Property owners benefit financially from constrained supply. The same local political systems that fail to produce sufficient housing stock are the systems in which property owners have successfully limited new construction for decades through zoning restrictions, environmental review processes, and ballot initiatives that require supermajority votes for density increases. The homelessness crisis is, in part, a downstream consequence of a housing market engineered to protect the asset value of existing owners.

The third force is moral distance. The physical geography of wealthy American cities permits millions of people to live without sustained contact with severe poverty. Freeways, hillside topography, gated developments, and commercial buffer zones separate the residential neighborhoods of the property-owning electorate from the corridors where encampments concentrate. This distance does not produce indifference through malice. It produces indifference through the absence of confrontation. A crisis that is experienced as an occasional visual disruption on a freeway off-ramp generates less political urgency than a crisis experienced as a permanent presence on one's own block.

The fourth force is institutional inertia. The existing homelessness services system in California employs thousands of people, distributes billions of dollars through established procurement channels, and sustains organizations whose institutional survival depends on the continuation of the current funding architecture. Changing the architecture disrupts all of that. Incumbent service providers possess the organizational capacity, the political relationships, and the procurement expertise to defend their position against any reform that threatens their contract base. This is not corruption. It is Olson's logic applied to a public services market. The incumbents are organized. The beneficiaries of reform are not.

Finland reduced its homeless population by roughly forty percent over fifteen years through a sustained national commitment to housing-led policy. The Finnish case does not transfer directly to Los Angeles, because Finland's welfare infrastructure, universal healthcare system, housing vacancy rates, and average duration of street homelessness at intake differ from California on every relevant dimension. What the Finnish case establishes is narrower and more important: that sustained political will, maintained across electoral cycles and applied consistently for over a decade, produces measurable results. The obstacle to solving street homelessness in the wealthiest society in human history is not capability. It is the political economy of inaction.

The technical correctness of an intervention does not produce its implementation. The medical consensus that cigarettes caused cancer existed decades before policy changed. The engineering solutions for lead contamination in municipal water systems were available long before governments replaced the pipes. The epidemiological evidence proving that needle exchange programs reduced HIV transmission was established years before most jurisdictions permitted them to operate. In each historical sequence, the distance between correct knowledge and public action was not closed by the publication of better papers. It was closed by a combination of forces that papers cannot generate. Visible crisis made inaction politically costly while organized constituencies applied sustained pressure. Legal mandates forced institutional response. Political entrepreneurs proved willing to absorb personal risk.

The working paper series on relational engineering outlines three operational claims that require this exact combination of forces to execute. First, the approach must match the neurological state of the person at the moment of contact. Second, the housing offer must never expire. Third, the survival friendships built on the street must survive the transition indoors.

Separating verified bond partners at the threshold of a housing facility recreates the neurological problem the facility was built to solve. On the street, a small network of trusted partners functions as the absolute baseline of physical safety. By assigning housing via random lottery or geographic availability, conventional intake systems sever those networks entirely. The individual arrives in a building full of strangers. Stripped of the relationships that previously provided biological regulation, the nervous system classifies the new environment as dangerous and immediately returns to a defensive state. The intervention fails. Material infrastructure remains present, yet the relational layer has been systematically destroyed.

To prevent this severance, the prototype employs a specific social network interview instrument administered in under five minutes. The protocol asks concrete questions regarding material reliance rather than subjective friendship. Workers ask individuals who watches their gear when they leave the tent, who regularly shares their food supply, and who they seek out first during a physical crisis. These answers generate a verified map of survival networks. The assignment algorithm then uses this map to mandate that mutually verified partners receive placement in the same residential pod. The process treats social connection not as a secondary preference, but as an engineering requirement for housing retention.

These algorithmic weights remain provisional. Field testing must calibrate the misclassification ceilings, and empirical observation must verify the minimum contact threshold for trust accumulation. The authors of the working papers acknowledge the limits of theoretical models. A prototype building functions as the specific instrument designed to generate that missing evidence.

Moving from paper to prototype requires specific structural levers. Those levers are currently available but uncoordinated. California’s managed care plans possess a financial incentive to fund relational interventions through CalAIM billing mechanisms, while the private capital market possesses an investment interest in the adaptive reuse of vacant commercial real estate. Simultaneously, the legal landscape applies continuous pressure through court decisions that prohibit the criminalization of unsheltered living unless adequate alternatives exist.

A working prototype coordinates these levers into a single visible demonstration. One commercial tower, operating under the relational protocols, measuring retention outcomes at ninety, one hundred and eighty, and three hundred and sixty-five days, constitutes the most powerful political argument available. The Finnish model persuaded other European nations not through the publication of academic theory, but through the production of visible outcomes. A prototype designed for political visibility forces other jurisdictions to observe the mechanism working in practice. A paper about organizing a coalition is not a coalition. The prototype is the required proof of concept.

Return to the woman on East Sixth Street. The outreach worker approached, the offer was made, and the refusal was recorded in the county database. The event was documented as a failure of compliance. It was actually a failure of design.

The offer was delivered to a defense reflex, not to a person. A genuine offer requires conditions under which it can be genuinely received. The wealth and the diagnostic tools already exist alongside the architectural models. The question is whether a society with more resources than any in human history decides that the people it has most completely failed deserve a system designed around their actual neurological reality, rather than a system engineered for the institutional convenience of the provider.

That choice is not a technical calculation. It represents a profoundly moral and political decision. The technical questions possess clear answers, leaving only the moral question open.

#### Socratic Audit

Physiological Contradiction: Baseline isolates dorsal-vagal shutdown. During chronic trauma, nervous systems cycle between sympathetic hyperarousal and parasympathetic collapse. Single-occupancy placement severs external threat-monitoring capacity. Without environmental feedback, subjects experience panic before entering terminal shutdown.

Financial Contradiction: Managed care systems fund clinical service delivery through standardized billing codes. Insurers cannot capitalize construction. To bypass rigid statutory restrictions, development models must integrate distinct financial instruments. Planners must separate physical architectural capital from operational service revenue.

Spatial Contradiction: Preserving survival networks requires clustered unit allocation. Because standard placement relies on randomized lotteries, scattering populations destroys established mutual defense structures.

#### Proposition 

Chronic street exposure forces human nervous systems into continuous cycles of hyperarousal and collapse. Since physiological extremis renders isolated housing biologically unintelligible, resolution demands structural separation. Capital streams require operational independence. By braiding distinct streams, systems sustain relational presence necessary to stabilize autonomic function while migrating intact defense networks indoors.
