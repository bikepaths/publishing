Traffic idles on Interstate 405. Marcus grips the steering wheel. Sun glare hits his windshield at twenty-three degrees, forcing him to squint against light that refuses to yield. The glass magnifies incoming radiation. Sweat pools along his collar. He flips the visor downward, checking his watch while inching toward the exit ramp. The dashboard clock indicates seven fifteen. He expects twilight. He expects shadow.

The planet defies him.

Concrete highways assume uniform rotation. Drivers rely on geometric consistency. The blazing sun shatters this illusion. The Earth behaves as a wobbling gyroscope, locking its northern axis toward the nearest star. Gravity overrides transit grids. At maximum tilt, atmospheric particles bend incoming light across latitudes, flooding the hemisphere with fifteen hours of punishing radiation. This sustained exposure suppresses human melatonin production instantly, disrupting circadian rhythms before anyone reaches home. A driver stuck in gridlock interprets this celestial friction as neurological warfare.

A delivery truck inches forward. Marcus hits the gas pedal. He advances ten feet. Fireflies signal from the roadside brush, confused by the lingering ultraviolet waves. The massive gyroscope maintains its lean. Tomorrow, gravity will begin pulling the axis backward, stealing microscopic fractions of daylight from the evening commute until autumn reclaims the highway. A cooling shadow will eventually return. Until then, the sun dominates the glass.
