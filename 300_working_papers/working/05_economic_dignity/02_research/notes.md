3. Social Belonging and Mutual Survival
Humans are deeply social creatures who crave connection and status.
Street Kinship: Homeless encampments often form deep, complex social structures. In these micro-communities, individuals find protection, shared resources, and a sense of belonging. They are viewed as human beings by their peers. [1 (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11730778/), 2 (https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/pdf/10.1080/01488376.2022.2050336)]
The Isolation of Four Walls: Moving an individual from a vibrant (though volatile) street community into a lonely, isolated efficiency apartment can cause a severe psychological crash. The sudden loss of social connection can feel like solitary confinement, driving people back to the streets simply to be around others.
4. Hyper-Focus on Present Survival (Hyperbolic Discounting)
When a person's nervous system is constantly in "fight or flight" mode, the brain's frontal lobe changes how it calculates time and value.
The Immediate Over the Distant: The brain focuses entirely on the immediate present—finding food for the next hour, or getting a blanket for the night.
The Housing Delay: The "pleasure" of a small, immediate comfort today (like a warm meal or a familiar sidewalk spot) heavily outweighs the promise of an economic pathway or a housing voucher that might take six months of confusing bureaucracy to achieve.

Housing First & Low-Barrier Housing: Provide private, permanent housing without immediate behavioral conditions. This replaces the street's autonomy and safety immediately, allowing the nervous system to calm down.
Community-Centric Transition: Build housing models that preserve social structures (like peer-support networks) so individuals do not suffer from sudden, severe isolation.
