# Manual of Style — Survival Physics Register
*For: The Moral Physics of Survival, Building Material Dignity, and future MDI general-audience volumes*
*Reader Profile: Educated general audience, effective Grade 10-11 reading level*
*Derived from: Empirical analysis of The Moral Physics of Survival manuscript, Chapters 1-13*

---

## Register

Witness-driven analytical prose. The author carries the authority of direct observation across three decades of street-level experience, combined with the intellectual rigor of named philosophical and scientific sources. The voice is serious, unhurried, and precise. It builds arguments through the accumulation of physical detail and allows the reader to arrive at conclusions rather than announcing them.

---

## Core Structural Mechanics

### 1. Concrete Before Abstract

Every clinical term, philosophical concept, or policy abstraction must be preceded by a physical image that the reader can see. The image delivers the reality; the term labels it. When the sequence is reversed, the prose reads like a textbook. When the sequence is correct, the prose reads like testimony.

*   **Correct:** Arthur sat on his plastic crate and watched the river for twelve hours without moving. He was in dorsal-vagal shutdown.
*   **Failure:** Dorsal-vagal shutdown is a condition in which the nervous system suspends voluntary activity. Arthur experienced this condition.

### 2. Scene-Then-Argument Structure

Chapters open with a physical scene, a historical event, or a specific observable fact. The scene anchors the reader in a particular place, body, or moment. The argument develops from the scene through progressive abstraction. The chapter closes by returning to the concrete or by delivering a single declarative sentence that carries the chapter's central claim.

### 3. The Long/Short Sentence Hammer

Complex sentences expand to 40-60 words when tracing causal chains, philosophical lineages, or institutional mechanics. These expansions earn their length through active verbs, subordinate clauses that carry genuine information, and the progressive narrowing of a logical argument. The expansion must be released by a short declarative hammer of 5-10 words. The rhythm is tension and release.

*   **Examples from manuscript:** "Arthur watched the river." / "The pump handle is removed." / "Personhood is not a performance." / "Then it didn't." / "So look again."

### 4. Opener Rotation

No more than half the sentences in any paragraph may open with the subject noun or pronoun. Valid openers include time markers, conditions, concessions, participles, prepositional phrases, and conjunctions. Monotonic subject-first construction produces prose that reads like a list of facts rather than a developing argument.

### 5. Syntactic Bridging

Establish structural flow through coordinate conjunctions and subordinate conjunctions linking genuine causal relationships. Do not use conjunctions as filler. Each "because," "since," "although," or "which" must introduce information the reader needs to follow the argument. Overuse of a single conjunction as a crutch signals that the sentences should be restructured.

### 6. Topic Sentence Primacy

The first sentence of every paragraph states the paragraph's central claim. Evidence, elaboration, and qualification flow from that declaration. The reader who scans first sentences alone should be able to reconstruct the chapter's argument.

## Voice and Narrative Architecture

### 7. Named Anchor Characters

At least one composite human subject recurs across chapters. This character carries the physical reality of the argument and prevents the prose from floating into pure abstraction. The character is observed, not psychologized. Their condition is described in clinical and physical terms. Their interiority is inferred from observable behavior rather than narrated.

*   **Manuscript precedent:** "Arthur" appears in the Prologue and recurs in Chapters 4, 5, 6, and 13. He is a composite of thirty years of observation.

### 8. Named Sources Woven Into Prose

Cite researchers, philosophers, and studies by name within the chapter text. The citation serves the argument as a weight-bearing structural element rather than an academic formality. The reader should feel that the argument stands on specific intellectual shoulders.

*   **Manuscript precedent:** Sapolsky, McEwen, Philippa Foot, Amartya Sen, Martha Nussbaum, John Snow, Deirdre McCloskey, Tyler Cowen, James Scott, Václav Havel, Hobbes, Locke, Rawls, Cicero, Grotius.

Do not use footnotes, endnotes, or inline parenthetical citations within chapter text. Full Chicago 17th edition bibliography entries appear in the Citations section at the end of the book.

### 9. First-Person Witness Authority

The author's direct experience is established in the Preface and Author's Note. Within chapters, the prose operates in third-person analytical voice. The first-person appears only when the authority of direct observation carries rhetorical force that third-person analytical voice cannot replicate. Use sparingly.

## Content and Tone Constraints

### 10. The Affirmative Mandate

State what a concept, mechanism, or outcome IS. Do not construct arguments through negation. "Not X but Y" structures are prohibited. The word "not" as a rhetorical contrast device is banned. Diagnostic negation identifying what a system fails to do is permitted when it describes an observable institutional failure.

### 11. Dollar Figures and Quantified Claims

Specific dollar amounts, population counts, retention percentages, and time horizons ground the argument in verifiable physical reality. Do not round to vague magnitudes. "Twenty-four billion dollars" carries more weight than "billions of dollars." "Forty percent retention" is an argument; "low retention" is an opinion.

### 12. Historical Analogies as Structural Arguments

Extended historical analogies serve as structural arguments rather than decorative comparisons. John Snow and the Broad Street pump operate across multiple chapters as a sustained metaphor for the method this book advocates. The analogy must bear analytical weight; if removing the analogy would not damage the argument, the analogy does not belong.

### 13. Sustained Tonal Commitment

The tone is serious, precise, and unhurried. It does not shift to accommodate the reader's discomfort. Sentimentality is prohibited. Moral urgency is conveyed through the accumulation of evidence rather than through rhetorical escalation. The prose trusts the reader to feel what the evidence implies without being told how to feel.

## Punctuation and Lexical Constraints

### 14. Punctuation Rules

*   **Em-dashes:** Prohibited. Express parenthetical thoughts through syntax.
*   **Parenthetical phrases:** Prohibited. State all information within the primary sentence structure. Acronym definitions on first occurrence are authorized.
*   **Colons:** Authorized only for formal lists or defining clauses. Not authorized as hard stops within sentences.
*   **Semicolons:** Authorized to link clauses with high causal parity.

### 15. Anti-Slop Index (Forbidden Vocabulary)

Cut on sight. No exceptions.

**Mechanical transitions:** furthermore, moreover, additionally, as a result, consequently, therefore, thus, hence, subsequently, finally, in summary, in conclusion, overall, ultimately, notably, importantly.

**Weak qualifiers:** actually, merely, simply, fundamentally, specifically, however, completely, absolutely, perfectly, entirely, explicitly, actively, smoothly, flawlessly, safely, securely, rigorously, violently, heavily, essentially, directly, certainly, of course, generally speaking, for the most part.

**Academic clichés:** utilize, utilization, provenance, nuanced, mitigate, synergy, paradigm, holistic, impactful, framework, leverage, facilitate, robust, seamless, comprehensive, vibrant, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, game-changer, groundbreaking, revolutionary, transformative, rich tapestry, foster, elevate, empower, ensure, stakeholders, actionable insights, best practices.

**Instructional filler:** to be clear, as mentioned, for example, in summary, in conclusion, note that, we must remember, as previously stated, before we dive in, it is helpful to understand, let us take a moment, first let us define, to better understand, let us explore, it is important to note, it is worth noting, keep in mind, feel free to.

**Intensity crutches:** exact, exactly, specific, specifically, direct, directly, precise, precisely, massive, massively, entire, entirely, complete, completely, highly, severe, severely, pure, purely, strict, strictly, constant, constantly.

### 16. Structural Purity

Chapters read as continuous narrative. No subheadings, bullet points, or numbered lists within chapter text. Transitions between narrative, clinical, economic, and philosophical sections occur through prose movement alone.

## Application Notes

The register produces its readability through the interplay of concrete observation and sustained argument rather than through simplified vocabulary or shortened sentences. A sentence that traces a causal chain across fifty words, grounded in a named researcher's finding and anchored to a dollar figure, reads more easily than a ten-word sentence that asserts a vague generality. The reader's trust is earned through specificity. The prose must repay that trust by never wasting the reader's attention on language that carries no information.

Write so that every paragraph teaches the reader something they did not know before they read it, and so that the teaching arrives through the physical evidence before the label is applied. The reader who finishes a chapter should feel that they have been shown something rather than told something.
