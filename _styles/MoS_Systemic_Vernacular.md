# Manual of Style: Systemic Vernacular (Analytical Journalism)

**Version:** 1.0
**Target Audience:** General adult readership, policy makers, and educated laypersons.
**Register Designation:** CEFR C1 (Advanced) / Flesch-Kincaid Grade 10-12.

## 1. Core Philosophy
The Systemic Vernacular style bridges the gap between the hyper-accessible "Kitchen Table" (OVP) and the clinical abstraction of Systemic Analysis. It is designed to introduce robust structural frameworks (e.g., incentive structures, epistemological shifts) without alienating readers through academic obfuscation. The tone should resemble high-tier analytical journalism (e.g., *The Atlantic, The Economist*).

## 2. Structural Bridging
This style relies on **Conceptual Anchoring**. Every abstract systemic mechanism introduced MUST be immediately grounded in a concrete, recognizable case study or historical precedent.

**Example of the Mechanism:**
- *Weak (OVP):* "The internet pays people to act crazy."
- *Weak (Systemic):* "The digital economy structurally incentivizes subclinical narcissistic performativity."
- *Strong (Systemic Vernacular):* "The modern attention economy actively monetizes interpersonal conflict, a dynamic clearly visible in the evolution of reality television and independent political commentators."

## 3. Lexical Constraints
While the vocabulary ceiling is raised to CEFR C1, strict constraints remain to prevent the descent into impenetrable academic jargon.

### 3.1 Permitted Analytical Terminology
Writers are encouraged to use precise structural language to describe complex phenomena:
- *Epistemology, commodification, incentive structures, plausible deniability, algorithmic distribution, institutional gatekeeping, ideological polarization.*

### 3.2 The Universal Banned Word List
The following words are globally prohibited to maintain narrative momentum, accessibility, and analytical purity:
*however, utilize, mitigate, robust, seamless, comprehensive, furthermore, moreover, additionally, therefore, thus, hence, absolutely, completely, structurally, operational, precisely, notably, generates, exact, exactly, strict, strictly, strickly, leverage, fosters, greatly, solely, nuanced, holistic, heavy, heavily, essential, fundamentally, specifically, perfectly, assets, symbiotic, dynamic, capacity, velocity, mechanisms.*

### 3.3 Prohibited Filler & Preaching Phrases
- "In conclusion", "In summary"
- "It is important to note that", "We must", "You should"
- "As mentioned above", "As stated previously"
- "etc." (Lists must be completed or the sentence restructured).

### 3.4 Global Syntactical and Punctuation Bans
- **Banned Punctuation:** Em-dashes (—), En-dashes (–), Semicolons (;), and Parentheses (). Colons may not be used as hard stops.
- **Binary Foils / Synthetic Contrast:** Do not use the "X is not Y. It is Z." or "not X, but Y" constructions.
- **Tricolons:** Avoid predictable "X, Y, and Z" three-item lists.
- **Staccato Phrasing:** Choppy, robotic sequences are explicitly forbidden.
- **Subject Pronoun Anaphora:** Never start more than two consecutive sentences with the same subject pronoun.
- **Rhetorical Closers:** Do not cluster short, manufactured declarative sentences at paragraph ends.

## 4. Syntactical Rhythm
- **Sentence Structure:** Compound-complex sentences are permitted and encouraged to sustain nuanced arguments.
- **Paragraph Architecture:** Maintain organic asymmetry. Mix shorter punchy paragraphs with longer analytical blocks. Do not adhere to the rigid clinical uniformity of the Systemic Analysis style.

## 5. Rhetorical Tone
- **Authoritative:** Speak directly and confidently.
- **Diagnostic:** Analyze the world as a series of interconnected systems and incentives, avoiding moralizing or emotional pleading.
- **Objective Distance:** Maintain a neutral, journalistic distance from the subjects being analyzed. Do not write from the perspective of an activist or a partisan.
