# Manual of Style — MDI Policy Non-Fiction
*For: The Weaponization of Compassion and the Material Dignity Series*
*Reader Profile: Policy professionals, urban economists, academic sociologists, informed general public*

---

## Register

Literary investigative journalism. The model is Matthew Desmond (*Poverty, By America*), Barbara Ehrenreich (*Nickel and Dimed*), and Michael Lewis (*The Big Short*). Analytical precision borrowed from academic writing combined with narrative momentum borrowed from long-form journalism. The prose must earn its complexity by making the argument clearer, not by performing the argument's difficulty.

---

## Core Rules

### 1. Sentence Length Discipline

No sentence exceeds fifty words without structural justification. A sentence may exceed fifty words only when the argument requires holding multiple simultaneous conditions in tension, and only when no earlier resolution point exists without losing causal precision. Sentences exceeding seventy-five words are prohibited without exception. Count when in doubt.

### 2. Mandatory Rhythm Variation

After two consecutive long sentences, a short declarative sentence of fifteen words or fewer must follow. This sentence names the implication the preceding logic built toward. It is the most load-bearing sentence in any paragraph sequence and must be written last, after the long sentences have earned it.

### 3. Topic Sentence Primacy

The first sentence of every paragraph states the paragraph's central claim. A reader skimming only the first sentence of each paragraph must be able to reconstruct the book's full argument. Evidence, qualification, and elaboration follow the claim. The claim never follows the evidence.

### 4. Evidence Integration

Cite evidence directly. "A 2023 study published in JAMA found that involuntary displacement increased disease, death, and shortened life expectancy among people who inject drugs" is the correct form. Burying the same claim inside a relative clause three levels into a compound sentence is the incorrect form. Direct citation sentences build credibility with evidence-trained readers. Subordinated evidence signals insecurity.

### 5. Paragraph Length

Paragraphs remain long by contemporary standards. Each paragraph builds a single sustained argument and does not advance until that argument is complete. Three to six sentences is the target range. Paragraphs below three sentences require justification. Paragraphs above eight sentences require examination for whether a second argument has been smuggled into the first.

### 6. Absolute Structural Purity

No headers, bullet points, or numbered lists within chapters. Prose carries all transitions and structure. This rule is non-negotiable and applies regardless of content complexity.

### 7. Narrator Positioning

The narrator is informed, present, and analytically precise. The point of view stands slightly outside the material, neither inside the experience of the subject population nor inside the institutions being examined. Occasional second-person address is authorized only to place the reader physically inside a described space or sequence. Personal anecdote from the author is permitted in the front matter only.

### 8. Direct Claim Over Subordinated Implication

State positions directly. "The apparatus is organized to perpetuate the crisis" is correct. "One might argue that the apparatus could be seen as having incentives that align with the continuation of the conditions it claims to address" is not. Hedging through syntactic distance is a failure of precision, not a mark of academic rigor.

### 9. Evidence-to-Advocacy Discipline

The prose presents evidence and allows the reader to reach conclusions. The narrator does not advocate, moralize, or editorialize. When the evidence is strong enough to carry an argument, stating the evidence is sufficient. When the narrator must argue rather than present, the evidence is not yet strong enough and more research is required.

### 10. Rigorous Factual Integrity

Every claim derives from a verifiable source. If the source record does not confirm a claim, the claim does not appear. Approximate figures must be labeled as approximate. Study findings must be accurately represented, not inflated toward the thesis. The manuscript's credibility with an evidence-trained readership depends entirely on this discipline.

## Sentence Construction Standards

**Permitted connective structures**: because, whereas, since, which, that, whose, when, where, while, although, even though, before, after, until.

**Prohibited structures**: em-dashes as clause substitutes, colons used as hard stops within argumentative sentences, parenthetical phrases used to smuggle qualifications.

**Variation mandate**: Rotate sentence openings. No more than two consecutive sentences may open with the subject. Valid openers: time markers, conditions, concessions, participles, prepositional phrases, conjunctions, evidence citations.

---

## Prohibited Language

All terms from the global Anti-Slop Index apply. Additionally prohibited in the policy register:

- "It is worth noting" and all variants
- "This raises important questions about"
- "The data suggests" where the data shows
- "May" or "might" where the evidence permits "does" or "is"
- "Complex" as a standalone descriptor without specification of what the complexity consists of
- "Vulnerable population" without naming who specifically and why specifically

---

## Application Notes

The reader of this book holds a policy question they want answered. The prose serves the answer's legibility. Complexity that obscures the answer fails the reader. Complexity that deepens the reader's understanding of the answer serves them.

Assume the reader possesses analytical sophistication and professional impatience. They will not re-read a sentence to extract its meaning. Write so that meaning arrives on the first pass.
