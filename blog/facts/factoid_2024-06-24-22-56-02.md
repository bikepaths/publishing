## Compiled Facts from Original Post
- The Invention Secrecy Act of 1952 is a United States federal law restricting the dissemination of new inventions and technologies that could jeopardize national security.
- The Patent and Trademark Office (PTO) and relevant defense agencies review patent applications to identify sensitive technology.
- A secrecy order prevents the applicant from publishing the patent, commercializing the technology, seeking investment, or collaborating on the work.
- The United States government provides compensation to applicants for maintaining secrecy orders.
- Secrecy orders can be lifted later through declassification, which allows the patent process to proceed.
- The act primarily impacts strategic commercial sectors like aerospace, defense, and advanced computing.

## Additional Research Facts
- Active Secrecy Orders: According to United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) statistics, the total number of active secrecy orders in effect rose from 5,976 in fiscal year 2021 to 6,543 at the end of fiscal year 2025.
- Order Influx and Outflow: In fiscal year 2025, the government imposed 102 new secrecy orders while rescinding only 30.
- Sponsoring Agencies: Secrecy orders are requested by defense and administrative agencies, including the Department of the Navy, Department of the Air Force, and Department of Energy (DOE).
- John Doe Orders: A subset of secrecy orders, known as John Doe orders, are imposed on private inventors who do not have government sponsorship or government interest in their inventions; 18 such orders were imposed during fiscal year 2025.
