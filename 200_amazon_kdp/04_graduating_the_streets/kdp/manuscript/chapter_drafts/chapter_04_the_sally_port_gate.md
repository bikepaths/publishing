# Chapter 4: The Sally Port Gate

The ambulance brought Marcus and Buster to the rear entrance of Tower One. Instead of a busy emergency room lobby filled with security guards and bright lights, the vehicle backed into a quiet, acoustic-walled sally port. Closing silently behind them, the metal gates blocked the city sirens and the rain. Here, the air was warm, and the lighting was soft and low, casting long shadows across the polished concrete floor. 

A veterinarian waited in the triage bay next to the entrance, ready to examine Buster. Rather than separating the dog from Marcus during intake, the intake design allowed both to remain in the same quiet room. While the veterinarian checked the dog's lungs and treated his persistent cough, Marcus sat in a wide armchair, watching every movement. To calm Marcus's racing pulse, the team avoided asking for forms or signatures, focusing on the dog's immediate physical comfort. 

Keeping Marcus and Buster together during the initial hours prevented a fight-or-flight defensive response. Under traditional shelter rules, pets are banned or kept in remote kennels, forcing owners to choose between shelter or abandonment. This separation triggers a severe panic response in a highly sensitive nervous system. At Tower One, the co-located veterinary node allowed Buster to sleep in a crate next to Marcus's bed, preserving the primary bond that kept Marcus grounded. 

This entrance protocol marks the transition from survival to decompression. By using soundproofing, dim lighting, and animal care, the ground floor space signals safety to a traumatized brain. Because he no longer had to defend his camp or search for food, Marcus allowed his body to rest. In this quiet room, his breathing slowed, and the long process of healing began.
