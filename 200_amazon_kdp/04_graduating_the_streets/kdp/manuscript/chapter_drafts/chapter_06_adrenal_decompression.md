# Chapter 6: Adrenal Decompression

After his initial medical treatment, Marcus moved to a private unit in the decompression wing. Faced with acoustic walls rated at sound transmission class sixty-five, the room blocked all traffic noise and hallway footsteps. The constant roar of the city faded away. For a person accustomed to sleeping in constant danger, this deep quiet felt unfamiliar, yet safe. 

To build safety, the room gave him control over his own door. Marcus received a keycard that locked the door from the inside, which prevented anyone from entering without his permission. Except during medical emergencies, staff members possessed no override key to his lock. Because he controlled the door, his constant threat-scanning behaviors began to fade. 

During his first week in the unit, Marcus slept for fourteen hours daily. His body, exhausted from years of constant vigilance on the riverbed, finally collapsed into a deep sleep. Because the threat of sweeps was gone, his adrenal glands rested. This prolonged rest operated as a form of medicine, allowing his brain to heal the damage of chronic survival. 

This decompression phase demonstrates that cognitive recovery cannot begin without physical safety. By shutting out the noise and threat of the street, the environment allowed Marcus's amygdala to quiet down. His mind started to clear in this silent sanctuary, preparing him for the next stage of his return.
