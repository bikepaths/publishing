# Chapter 8: Hyper-Vigilance Extinction

Even inside the quiet unit, Marcus struggled with the physical habits of survival. For years, his body had reacted to every sudden sound as a threat. When walking down the corridors, he kept his back to the wall to watch the corners. To help his nervous system unlearn these automated reactions, the building featured textured wood panels along the hallways. Touching these natural surfaces grounded him in the present. 

Weekly grounding exercises with his outreach worker focused on physical sensations rather than traumatic memories. Instead of asking him to talk about the riverbed, the worker guided him to identify the texture of the carpet or the weight of a weighted blanket. By proving his current surroundings were safe, these physical inputs calmed his overactive amygdala. As his body learned that it was no longer under attack, his breathing slowed and his muscles relaxed. 

Buster also participated in these soothing routines. When Marcus felt panic rising, the dog would rest his head on Marcus's knee, providing a familiar warmth that broke the spiral of anxiety. This mutual comfort helped both man and dog recover from the chronic stress of the streets. Through these shared moments, they rebuilt their sense of safety together. 

Extinguishing hyper-vigilance requires consistent physical evidence of safety over months. By replacing threats with predictable, soothing sensations, the program helped Marcus's nervous system step down from high alert. Grounding the body in physical reality remains the necessary first step toward restoring the damaged mind. When the threat-response system rests, cognitive healing begins.
