# Chapter 11: The Stewardship Model

After months of rebuilding his own life, Marcus took on a leadership role. Recovery reaches its full potential when a resident shifts from receiving assistance to offering support to others. To support this final stage of growth, the program trained experienced residents to serve as guides and mentors for incoming clients. Marcus welcomed new arrivals. 

As a peer steward, Marcus helped incoming clients navigate their first weeks. He showed them where to get meals, how to use keycards, and where to take pets for veterinary checkups. Sharing his own history built immediate trust with panicked newcomers. Because he had survived the Glendale Narrows riverbed himself, he spoke with an authority that staff members could never match. 

Inside the workshop, Marcus led the weekly bicycle maintenance class. Teaching others how to repair tires and adjust brakes gave him a sense of social responsibility. Watching incoming peers master these repair skills allowed Marcus to see the physical evidence of his own recovery progress reflected in their labor. This role built workplace habits. 

The stewardship model demonstrates that lasting recovery requires a path toward social contribution. By training former clients to guide new arrivals, the program builds a sustainable network of peer support. Leadership restored Marcus's place in the community. When a person helps others heal, they finish their own recovery.
