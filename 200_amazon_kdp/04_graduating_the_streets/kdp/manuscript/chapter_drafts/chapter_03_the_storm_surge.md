# Chapter 3: The Storm Surge

A sudden winter storm in early November turned the shallow riverbed into a brown torrent. Within twelve hours, rainwater running off concrete city streets surged into the Glendale Narrows, rising four feet to drown the mud banks. When the rushing water swept away his spare tarps, Marcus scrambled to a high patch of mud. Clinging to willow branches, he held the dog's head above the rising current. 

From the concrete embankment above, the outreach team saw the rising flood. Because they had spent weeks visiting his camp, Marcus recognized them as friends instead of hostile intruders. They shouted across the roaring channel, offering safety inside Tower One. To ease his panic, the workers promised that Buster would stay with him, pointing to a veterinary crate at the top of the slope. 

Faced with the choice between freezing in the riverbed or leaving his dog behind, Marcus froze. The roar of the rushing river and the freezing rain overwhelmed him, locking his body in a state of numb paralysis. Through the noise, the shouts from the bank faded away. To break this silence, one of the workers waded into the cold water, carrying a leash and a sturdy plastic crate for Buster. By focusing on saving the dog first, the worker coaxed Marcus out of his panic and guided both man and shivering dog up the slippery concrete wall. 

This rescue showed that helping someone in crisis requires working with what they love. Instead of using police sweeps or jail threats to clear the riverbed, the program used the flood as a moment to offer help. Because the outreach team protected the dog first, they proved they respected what Marcus valued most. This act of care turned a life-threatening storm into the day Marcus finally left the riverbed behind.
