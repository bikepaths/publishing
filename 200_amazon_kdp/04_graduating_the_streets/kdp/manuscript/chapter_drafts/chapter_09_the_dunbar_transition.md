# Chapter 9: The Dunbar Transition

After two months of individual decompression, Marcus prepared for the next step. Moving straight into a crowded public housing project often triggers intense panic. To prevent this psychological setback, the architectural design divided the residents into independent pods limited to one hundred fifty people. This group size matches human limits for social connection. 

Inside Pod Eight, Marcus shared cooking and dining areas with a few neighbors. The peer steward introduced him to his suite neighbors during his first morning on the floor. Rather than forcing him to face a loud cafeteria filled with hundreds of strangers, the program introduced him to four neighbors in a quiet room. Because daily interactions occurred within these small clusters, the design protected Marcus from the social panic of large congregate shelters. 

As he grew comfortable, Marcus took on shared duties. Assisting with kitchen cleaning and laundry schedules gave him a daily routine and a sense of purpose. During weekly meetings, residents discussed communal rules and resolved conflicts together. This cooperative management restored the personal agency that years of street survival had eroded. 

Transitioning to a residential pod demonstrates that social reintegration must scale to human limits rather than forcing people into massive groups. By dividing the building into small units, the design avoids the stress of congregate halls. Marcus rebuilt his connections through peer support. Cooperating with neighbors restored his ability to trust.
