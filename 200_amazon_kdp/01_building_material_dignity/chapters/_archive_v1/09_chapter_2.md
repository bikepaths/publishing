# Chapter 2: The Nuclear Isolate

Before the economic boom after World War Two, large families living together were the main engine for human survival. Most people lived near their grandparents and cousins because they needed each other to get by. This closeness provided a local welfare system that worked for everyone. It absorbed the shocks of job loss or sickness since the relatives shared the burden. The large family was the original safety net that kept people safe from the friction of life.

After the war, the country built a new map. The government paid for the growth of suburbs and pushed workers to move far away for jobs. This physical shift broke the large family apart as relatives scattered across the map. By 1980, large family households hit a record low. The new system replaced the big network with the small nuclear family of just parents and children. The old rules told everyone that this small, isolated unit was the new standard for success.

The nuclear family is a strange way to live that only worked for a short time. It survived because the economy possessed massive surplus capital and federal subsidies following the war. This small unit has no structural weight because it is too fragile. A home with only two adults cannot absorb a major disaster. If one parent loses a job or gets sick, the whole family faces immediate ruin. The household has no nearby relatives to share the costs or the work. The nuclear family acts as a weak island in a stormy sea.

The modern street tent is the direct result of this isolation. When the economy changed in the late 1970s, the small family broke under the pressure of rising prices. People who lost their jobs found no backup network to catch them. The old system expected the small family to do the work of a massive network whereas that idea was mathematically false. The result of this bad math is visible on every city street. The person on the sidewalk is the physical output of a machine that tore down its safety nets. We replaced them with weak units that cannot survive hard times.
