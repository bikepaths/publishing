# Chapter 16: The Legal Lever

The hardest problem on the street involves people who do not know they are sick. The human brain can break in a specific way where it loses the ability to see its own damage. Doctors call this condition anosognosia. A person with this damage will stand in traffic or sleep in freezing rain while believing they are fine. They will refuse any offer of a warm bed or a doctor because their brain tells them they do not need help.

The old system makes a fatal mistake with this group. The current rules force the city to wait until the sick person asks for help. This wait is endless because the broken brain will never make that request. The city watches these people die on the sidewalk while claiming their hands are tied by the law. Leaving a person on the street because they are too sick to understand their own danger is not freedom. It is neglect.

To fix this failure, the city must use the law as a physical tool. We call this the Legal Lever. When a person is broken by the street, a judge must have the power to force them inside. New legal rules like CARE Court and conservatorship give the state this exact power. These tools allow a judge to pull a person off the sidewalk and place them into the locked medical floors of our towers. 

This forced move is not a punishment since the person is not going to jail. They are going to a secure hospital floor built for their specific pipeline. The judge uses the Legal Lever to save the person's life when their own brain has failed them. The public supports this action because the alternative is letting sick people slowly die in public.

The Legal Lever is the final piece of the machine. The towers provide the physical rooms, the airlocks provide the quiet, and the pods provide the community. For the people who are too sick to walk through the front door on their own, the law provides the push. Once the city connects the new towers to this legal power, the system is complete. The machine will finally have the strength to pull everyone inside and clear the streets for good.
