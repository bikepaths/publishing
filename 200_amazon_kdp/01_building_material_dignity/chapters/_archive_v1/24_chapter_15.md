# Chapter 15: The Dunbar Pod

The human brain has a hard limit on how many people it can trust. Scientists call this Dunbar’s Number. The rule states that a person can only maintain stable relationships with about one hundred and fifty people. Beyond this number, the brain cannot remember faces or track who is safe. When a person is forced into a crowd larger than this limit, they feel stressed and defensive. The old system ignores this biological rule. 

Large city shelters often pack five hundred or a thousand people into a single warehouse. This massive scale creates a constant state of fear. A person in a giant shelter is surrounded by hundreds of strangers they do not know and cannot trust. The brain stays on high alert because the crowd is too large to understand. This environment leads to theft and violence because there is no natural community to keep the peace. The huge shelter fails because it breaks the natural math of the human brain.

Our project fixes this error by using the math of the towers. We divide the massive skyscrapers into small, separate neighborhoods. Every floor of the tower is locked and holds exactly one hundred and fifty people. We call this a Dunbar Pod. When a person leaves the Phase Zero airlock, they move into one of these pods. They get their own private room on a floor where they will quickly learn everyone's name.

This small scale changes everything. Inside a Dunbar Pod, you recognize every face in the hallway. If a stranger enters the floor, the community notices immediately. The people on the floor begin to act like a tribe rather than a crowd of strangers. They look out for each other and keep their shared spaces clean. The city does not need to force rules on the pod because natural human trust takes over. The small size creates a safe village inside the steel frame of the tower.

Building vertically allows the city to scale this solution. A single tower can hold dozens of these small villages stacked on top of each other. The city gets the massive numbers it needs to clear the streets while the individual gets the small community they need to feel safe. The Dunbar Pod proves that we do not have to choose between helping a lot of people and treating them well. We can do both by following the physical rules of human nature.
