# Chapter 3 Atomic Facts

## Section 1: Expenditure Tracking and Funding Models
- Between 2019 and 2026, California spent twenty-four billion dollars on crisis management.
- State auditors found that agencies counted hot meals, emergency beds, and temporary supply distributions.
- Agencies did not monitor long-term housing retention.
- The state funded organizations based on the number of bed-nights filled.
- The funding model incentives organizations to avoid measuring housing stability.

## Section 2: Volume-Based Service Models
- Governments outsource services to external organizations.
- Outsourced organizations are paid based on volume of service rather than long-term outcomes.
- Under volume-based contracts, nonprofits count meal distributions rather than hunger reduction.
- Emergency shelters, outreach vans, and hygiene programs operate under volume-based contracting models.
- These organizations rely on unresolved distress to maintain their operations.

## Section 3: Shelter Operations and Regional Comparison
- State funding supported emergency shelters that require residents to leave by six in the morning.
- Early morning exit requirements disrupt resident sleep cycles and limit employment opportunities.
- Outreach vans distribute survival supplies on the street without placing individuals in housing.
- Between 2011 and 2020, Houston redirected its budget to permanent supportive housing placements.
- Houston used a unified Homeless Management Information System database to coordinate placements.
- Houston achieved a sixty percent reduction in its unsheltered population between 2011 and 2020.

## Section 4: Lobbying and Data Reporting
- Large temporary service contractors oppose permanent housing initiatives.
- These organizations receive millions of dollars in annual funding.
- Contractor lobbyists argue that permanent housing is unrealistic, too expensive, and unsuited to the population.
- Contractors record and present individual contacts as evidence of service.
- Long-term housing outcomes are not reported to the public.

## Section 5: Opposition to Outcome-Based Funding
- Service providers oppose outcome-based contracting that links payments to verified housing placements.
- Providers organize political opposition against outcome-based metrics.
- Public arguments by providers assert that outcome-based metrics are administratively complex and unfair.
- USDA research in 2023 and HUD research in 2022 confirm that shelter refusal is driven by separation from pets, partners, or possessions.
