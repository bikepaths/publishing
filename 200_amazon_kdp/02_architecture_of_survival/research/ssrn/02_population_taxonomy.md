# 02_POPULATION_TAXONOMY: The Four-Pipeline Framework
## Material Dignity Infrastructure — Clinical Profiles and Behavioral Stratification
*Feeds: Section on Target Population Classification in the manuscript.*

---

### Overview

The chronic unsheltered population is not a monolithic actor. MDI interventions fail when they treat all unsheltered individuals as a single cohort requiring a single solution. These four pipelines function as a dynamic state machine rather than a static categorization; consequently, while structurally distinct clinical profiles exist, individuals may transition between them as behavioral stability and psychiatric acuity fluctuate. Misallocating instrument to current state produces waste at best, compounded harm at worst.

### The Dynamic State Machine
The MDI taxonomy recognizes that homelessness is a non-linear process. A Pipeline B resident may regress to a Pipeline C state during a substance-induced crisis; conversely, a Pipeline C individual may stabilize into a Pipeline A state following metabolic intervention in Phase Zero. The MDI tracking architecture logs these transitions in the by-name registry to ensure that clinical instruments—such as ACT intensity or legal lever eligibility—adjust automatically to the individual's current state. By anticipating these transitions, the framework prevents instrument mismatch and maintains systemic responsiveness throughout the engagement window.

### Pipeline A: The Near-Homeless and Voluntarily Transitioning

**Profile**: Economic disruption, temporary crisis, or institutional discharge (hospital, prison, foster care). No significant psychiatric comorbidity or behavioral calcification. Social functioning largely intact.

**Behavioral Pattern**: Self-identifies as needing housing. Engages voluntarily with Coordinated Entry System (CES). Completes intake documentation. Responds to case management within standard timescales.

**Intervention**: Rapid document preparation, Housing First unit placement, standard case management. The MDI ALMU model resolves this cohort with minimal friction. CES is the existing pipeline; MDI accelerates it by creating surplus inventory.

**Scale Note**: This population, while significant in number, is not the primary driver of street visibility or public safety concern. They cycle through faster and rarely form long-term encampments.

---

### Pipeline B: The Encamped but Engageable

**Profile**: Long-term encampment with functional social bonds within the camp community. May have psychiatric comorbidity but retains enough insight to evaluate offers rationally. Refuses *congregate* shelter specifically.

**The "Three Ps" Refusal Pattern**: Research (USDA, 2023; HUD, 2022) consistently identifies three stated reasons for shelter refusal in this cohort:
- **Pets**: Cannot bring animal companions. Pets function as primary emotional regulation and survival anchor.
- **Partners**: Forced separation from intimate partners or close companions.
- **Possessions**: Loss of accumulated belongings — tools, documents, identity objects — that represent the individual's only remaining assets.

**Critical Finding**: "Shelter refusal" in Pipeline B is almost never refusal of housing itself. It is refusal of a specific model with specific barriers. Eliminate the barriers and acceptance rates climb sharply.

**Intervention**: The MDI ALMU model directly dissolves all three stated barriers: private unit (hard door, keycard), on-site pet accommodation (kennel/veterinary nodes), secure possession storage, and partner co-location within the same Dunbar Pod. Houston's "The Way Home" achieved 58-70% encampment-level acceptance rates when specific, immediate offers were made. MDI's architectural model is superior to what Houston offered.

**Engagement Timeline**: Weeks to months. Sustained presence outreach followed by a specific warm offer at an acute crisis window.

### Pipeline C: The Behaviorally Calcified Chronic

**Profile**: The core of the street crisis and the primary driver of public safety concern. Long Duration of Untreated Psychosis (DUP: 5-15 years). Active schizophrenia spectrum disorder, often with Diogenes syndrome overlay. Anosognosia prevents insight into illness. Paranoid or delusional systems make sustained engagement feel threatening.

**Clinical Mechanisms**:

*Anosognosia*: Neurological incapacity to perceive one's own psychiatric condition. Not denial. Not defiance. The frontal lobe damage producing psychosis simultaneously destroys the self-monitoring faculty that would recognize it. Up to 50% of individuals with schizophrenia present with anosognosia. Outreach strategies premised on persuasion target a faculty that has been metabolically destroyed.

*Duration of Untreated Psychosis (DUP)*: Each year of untreated active psychosis produces measurable progressive brain changes — increasing cognitive rigidity, treatment resistance, and functional decline. A decade of street-level untreated psychosis is a different neurological substrate than six months. Standard clinical timescales do not apply.

*Diogenes Syndrome Overlay*: Extreme self-neglect, domestic squalor (fecal accumulation, hoarded discards), social withdrawal, and adamant refusal of help. The accumulated hoard is not random accumulation. It functions as what the MDI framework terms an Exoskeletal Identity Structure — the physical boundary of a self-constructed world that has replaced the social world that rejected or terrified the individual. Removing the hoard without replacing the identity structure triggers acute psychiatric destabilization. Intervention must be identity-preserving.

*Metabolic Collapse as Cognitive Impairment*: The individual presenting at intake is not operating from a healthy neurological baseline. Electrolyte derangements (sodium, potassium, magnesium) from sustained dehydration and malnutrition directly impair executive function, impulse control, and emotional regulation. Volatility at intake is partly physiology, not exclusively psychiatry.

**Behavioral Pattern**: Active hostility to outreach. Conspiratorial framing of offers. Repeated encampment re-establishment following displacement. Preference for outdoor environment because the paranoid threat-perception system has habituated to it — unfamiliar enclosed spaces trigger acute agitation. Multi-actor escalation scenarios common in dense encampments.

**Intervention**: ACT (Assertive Community Treatment) sustained presence over 12-24 months. CARE Court/AOT/LPS legal lever system for individuals who cannot be voluntarily engaged. Specific, immediate warm offer at acute crisis window. See `03_field_architecture.md` and `04_legal_lever_system.md` for full mechanics.

**Critical Constraint**: Encampment sweeps without simultaneous housing availability produce only geographic redistribution for this cohort. Sweeps sever ACT outreach relationships and extend engagement timelines by months. Clearance is only viable *after* housing is pre-matched and the warm offer is ready to execute.

### Pipeline C (Sub-Variant): The Hidden/Nomadic Riparian Population

**Profile**: A sub-set of Pipeline C that is invisible to standard PIT counts and actively resistant to both voluntary outreach and legal lever engagement. Mobile, terrain-adaptive, and encamped in environmentally sensitive or legally inaccessible sites: LA River riparian corridor, Ballona Creek, Santa Ana River, Hansen Dam Recreation Area, storm drain infrastructure, and urban edge creek systems. Estimated at 20-30% above the PIT count's Pipeline C figure due to systematic undercount of hidden sites.

**The Encampment as Functioning System**: This population has built a calibrated survival architecture:
- Territorial stability: known, defended, habituated.
- Mutual protection hierarchies within the encampment social structure.
- Known supply chains for food, cigarettes, alcohol, and harm reduction materials.
- Animal companions as primary attachment bonds and territorial co-habitees.
- Freedom from institutional scheduling, surveillance, and compliance demands.
- Ontological security derived from competence in terrain survival, not from comfort.

The MDI voluntary offer competes against a system that already operates for this population on its own terms. It wins on permanent shelter, elimination of theft risk, medical access, and the no-rousting guarantee. It loses on unrestricted smoking, in-unit animal co-habitation, and social freedom from institutional rules. The voluntary acceptance rate for this sub-population is 15-30% globally across all evidence-based programs regardless of offer quality.

**Environmental Impact**: Riparian encampments produce documented federal regulatory violations that transform this from a social services problem into an environmental enforcement matter:
- Fecal coliform bacteria contamination of LA waterways at levels exceeding Clean Water Act NPDES permit standards.
- Hypodermic needle and pharmaceutical waste infiltration into riparian and wetland habitat.
- Biological waste and trash accumulation blocking flood control infrastructure (Army Corps jurisdiction).
- Destruction of native riparian vegetation and wildlife habitat in protected corridor zones.
LARWQCB enforcement records document these violations. They provide a legal basis for abatement independent of housing law and not governed by the *City of Grants Pass v. Johnson* (2024) framework.

**The FLIR Enforcement Track (Recommended, Not Currently Operational)**:
- Systematic FLIR thermal drone mapping of riparian corridors at night. Body heat signatures are unambiguous against cool riparian vegetation. Navigable airspace observation requires no warrant (*Dow Chemical v. United States*, 1986; *Florida v. Riley*, 1989).
- Cost: UAV thermal imaging can map miles of riparian corridor per flight hour at several hundred dollars operational cost.
- GIS integration identifies encampment locations, approximate population density, and mobility patterns for ACT team targeting.
- Environmental abatement proceedings (LARWQCB, Army Corps, County Public Works) trigger simultaneously with the MDI warm housing offer. The legal basis for clearance is environmental protection; the MDI inventory provides the simultaneous placement that makes the clearance durable.
- This track is not currently operational in Los Angeles. The Santa Ana River County cleanup (2018) is the closest precedent. Systematic FLIR-assisted riparian mapping and coordinated environmental-housing enforcement is a recommended MDI infrastructure addition requiring inter-agency coordination.

**Intervention**: Environmental compliance enforcement as the access mechanism. ACT team presence where terrain permits. FLIR-assisted location. Simultaneous warm offer at abatement proceeding. For individuals who refuse placement at abatement: LPS Conservatorship if grave disability criteria are met.

### Pipeline D: The Voluntary Nomadic

**Profile**: Deliberately self-organized outdoor life. Not metabolically destabilized in the Pipeline C clinical sense. Functioning within their own terms — informal economy, community bonds, deliberate rejection of institutional structures. No active psychiatric crisis requiring legal intervention.

**Distinction from Pipeline C**: Pipeline D individuals are not calcified by untreated psychosis. The choice to remain outside institutional structures is volitional and coherent, not driven by anosognosia or metabolic collapse. This distinction is clinically and legally material — no legal instrument applies to Pipeline D. Compelled pathways are not authorized.

**Behavioral Pattern**: Stable presence in known locations. Often functional supply chains and social hierarchies. Less visible as a public safety emergency than Pipeline C. Rational refusal of housing that requires permanent institutional affiliation.

**Environmental Effect of Pipeline C Housing**: When Pipeline C is substantially housed (the 70–80% reduction target), the remaining street population is qualitatively Pipeline D. Smaller, less acutely destabilized, less visible as a public safety emergency. Behavioral contagion from dense encampment conditions diminishes. Pipeline D individuals become more reachable by harm reduction services and more likely to self-select into ground floor open access over time.

**Intervention**: Harm reduction without housing requirement. No legal instrument. Ground floor open access Resource Center provides voluntary engagement point. Over time, as the MDI offer quality (Three Ps architecture, specific pre-matched unit) becomes established in community knowledge, voluntary self-selection rates in this cohort are expected to rise.

### The Population Calculus for MDI Planning

| Pipeline | Engagement Timeline | Primary Intervention | MDI Node |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| A — Voluntary | Days to weeks | CES rapid placement | Ground Floor → ALMU direct |
| B — Engageable | Weeks to months | Warm offer at crisis window | Field Team → ALMU |
| C — Calcified (Visible) | 12–24 months + legal track | ACT + CARE Court + AOT/LPS | Field Team → Legal Lever → ALMU |
| C — Hidden/Riparian | Abatement + simultaneous offer | FLIR mapping + environmental enforcement + LPS | LARWQCB/Army Corps → Warm Offer → ALMU |
| D — Voluntary Nomadic | Indefinite / self-selected | Harm reduction + open access Resource Center | Resource Center → Self-selected ALMU |

**Voluntary Participation Ceiling**: Pipeline A approaches full voluntary engagement. Pipeline B reaches 58-70% (Houston precedent). Pipeline C visible: 15-30% voluntary, remainder via legal lever. Pipeline C hidden: minimal voluntary, environmental enforcement is the primary access mechanism. The legal and environmental tracks exist because they are necessary, not because the voluntary track failed.
