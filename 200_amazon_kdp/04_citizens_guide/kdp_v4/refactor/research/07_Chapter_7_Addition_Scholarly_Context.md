## 2. Chapter 7 Addition: Situating MDI within the Scholarly Context
*(The following paragraph is drafted for direct insertion into Chapter 7 to clarify MDI's position relative to prior academic capability translations.)*

While this manuscript translates Nussbaum’s capabilities into concrete architectural and operational mandates, it is not the first attempt to bridge the Capability Approach with complex systems engineering. The translation of human capabilities to infrastructure design has previously been explored in the context of critical infrastructure prioritization, notably by Clark, Seager, and Chester (2018), who utilized the approach to rank the criticality of physical assets during disaster recovery. More recently, Aguilera, Osman, and Curto (2025) have integrated the Capability Approach into agent-based computational models to simulate homelessness policy outcomes. However, the Material Dignity Infrastructure (MDI) framework diverges fundamentally from both precedents. MDI does not seek to rank existing infrastructure or computationally model theoretical policy outcomes; rather, it is a unified physical delivery system. MDI functions as an active industrial architecture—utilizing biological thresholds, specific neuro-architectural geometry, and contiguous clinical pipelines to physically manufacture physiological stability on the ground. It is an execution engine, not an academic prioritization tool or a simulation framework.
