# Chapter 20: State Funding and Medicaid

## Chapter 20: State Funding and Medicaid

The Medicaid 1115 Waiver is a provision of the federal Medicaid statute that allows states to apply to the federal Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services for permission to use Medicaid funds for services and populations not traditionally covered by standard Medicaid. In other words, it allows states that want to be more innovative about how they use Medicaid dollars to get federal approval for that innovation.

California’s implementation of the 1115 Waiver framework, known as CalAIM, California Advancing and Innovating Medi-Cal, is the most ambitious such implementation in the country. It represents a fundamental reframing of how California treats chronic homelessness: not as a law enforcement problem, not as a social services problem, but as a healthcare crisis eligible for healthcare funding.

Enhanced Care Management and In Lieu of Services

CalAIM establishes two specific billing categories that are central to the MDI operational revenue model.

Enhanced Care Management generates billing when a resident achieves metabolic clearance, the biological milestone described in Chapter 15. ECM is a Medicaid-billable care coordination service for individuals with complex needs, and the clinical monitoring and coordination work that produces and documents metabolic clearance qualifies for ECM billing under CalAIM. This means that for every MDI resident who achieves metabolic clearance, a clinical outcome that the system is designed to produce, the facility generates Medicaid revenue.

In Lieu of Services generates billing for retention in supportive housing beyond 30 days. ILOS is a CalAIM provision that allows Medicaid to pay for housing-adjacent services, including the kind of supportive housing environment that the Dunbar Pod provides, as an alternative to more expensive clinical interventions. An MDI resident who remains stably housed in a Dunbar Pod beyond 30 days is generating ILOS revenue, because the supported housing is functioning as an alternative to the emergency room, psychiatric hospitalization, and incarceration that would otherwise be the likely outcomes.

The Billing Integrity Requirement

The dual-trigger structure of ECM and ILOS creates a potential perverse incentive that the MDI model explicitly guards against: if clearance and retention both generate billing, a financially motivated system might delay clearance in order to maximize retention billing, or rush clearance in order to maximize ECM billing. Neither is acceptable.

The Billing Integrity Requirement is the protocol that prevents this: clearance decisions are made exclusively by clinical staff based on clinical indicators, with no connection to billing state. The billing system is downstream of the clinical system and has no authority to influence clinical decisions. Any situation where both ECM and ILOS billing are triggered simultaneously, which happens when a resident achieves clearance while still in retention, generates a dual-trigger alert requiring independent review to ensure that the clinical decision was clinically motivated.

This safeguard is not bureaucratic overhead. It is the protection that makes the billing model trustworthy. A system where clinical decisions are influenced by billing incentives is a system that cannot be trusted to produce genuine clinical outcomes.

National Equivalents

CalAIM is a California-specific implementation. Other states have or can pursue equivalent 1115 Waiver structures, with varying degrees of ambition and coverage. The MDI billing architecture is designed around the general federal Medicaid framework; CalAIM is the California implementation layer, not the core model. Any state pursuing MDI outside California needs to map its own 1115 Waiver structure (or pursue one if it does not yet exist) to the ECM and ILOS equivalents.

The federal incentive structure for states to pursue 1115 Waivers is strong: federal Medicaid matching funds are available for approved waiver programs, meaning that state dollars spent on waiver-approved services generate additional federal revenue. A state that invests in pursuing a robust 1115 Waiver for housing-adjacent services can generate significant federal matching funds for MDI operational costs.

California State Capital Programs

Beyond CalAIM, California has several state capital programs that can contribute to the MDI construction financing stack. Proposition 1, passed by California voters in March 2024, authorizes $6.38 billion in general obligation bonds for mental health infrastructure and housing, with significant portions specifically designated for the most severely mentally ill. The No Place Like Home program uses Mental Health Services Act funds for the development of permanent supportive housing for individuals experiencing homelessness with serious mental illness. CARE Act funding supports the civil care court pathway described in Chapter 16.

These programs are California-specific, but they illustrate the range of state-level capital that can complement federal financing. Other states have analogous programs at varying levels of development and funding, and MDI advocates in any state should map the available state capital programs as part of the capital stack assembly process.
