# Chapter 7: The Capability Approach

## Chapter 7: The Capability Approach

In 1998, the economist Amartya Sen received the Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences for work that included a fundamental reframing of what it means to be free. Standard economic models had long treated freedom as a matter of resources: a person is free to the degree that they have money and legal rights. Sen argued that this framing missed something essential. A person who is legally free to attend university but who has never learned to read is not, in any meaningful sense, free to attend university. A person who is legally free to choose where they live but whose brain has been damaged beyond the capacity to navigate a housing application is not, in any meaningful sense, free to choose where they live.

Real freedom, Sen argued, requires both the internal capacity and the external conditions to exercise it. He called these capabilities, the opportunities to live and act in ways you have reason to value. Policy that provides resources without capabilities is not providing freedom. It is providing the appearance of freedom to people who cannot use it.

This distinction, between the formal existence of a right or resource and the actual ability to use it, is the philosophical foundation of the MDI model. It is why MDI begins with the body before the building, and with the brain before the lease. A housing key provided to someone whose brain cannot maintain a tenancy is not a housing solution. It is a gesture that will produce failure and then be used as evidence that the person did not want help.

Nussbaum’s Human Capabilities List

Martha Nussbaum, working in the same philosophical tradition as Sen, developed his framework into a specific list of central human capabilities that she argued every society is obligated to guarantee at a meaningful threshold. Her list includes ten capabilities, ranging from political participation to play to emotional development. Three of them are foundational, the capabilities that all others depend on and without which none of the others can be meaningfully exercised.

Life: the ability to live a normal-length human life, to not die prematurely. Bodily Health: the ability to have good health, including reproductive health; adequate nutrition; and adequate shelter. Bodily Integrity: the ability to move freely from place to place, to be secure against violent assault, to have opportunities for sexual satisfaction and for choice in matters of reproduction.

These three capabilities are precisely what the street destroys. The chronically unsheltered person has been denied bodily health through nutritional deprivation and sleep deprivation. They have been denied bodily integrity through exposure to predation and violence, since research on encampment safety consistently documents extremely high rates of violent victimization among chronically unsheltered people, particularly among women and people with psychiatric disabilities. And their life, in the full sense that Nussbaum intends, a life lived with dignity and meaning and reasonable duration, has been reduced to biological survival against compounding physiological odds.

A society that claims to value human capabilities while building cities that systematically deny them to the most vulnerable is engaged in a fundamental contradiction. Not a regrettable one. A fundamental one. The city is asserting that it values the dignity and freedom of all its residents while creating and maintaining the physical conditions that make dignity and freedom impossible for the most exposed of them. The MDI model is the engineering specification that takes that assertion seriously enough to act on it.

The Sequential Resolution

The MDI model structures the insights of Sen and Nussbaum into a chronological sequence that resolves what might otherwise appear to be a tension in the philosophical framework. If capabilities require both internal capacity and external conditions, what do you do when a person has lost the internal capacity, when the brain has been damaged by the street beyond the point of being able to use external resources even when they are provided?

The question is not hypothetical. This is precisely the situation of the C-pipeline population. They have lost, through no failure of character or will, the neurological infrastructure that would allow them to use a housing voucher or maintain a tenancy or navigate a service system. Providing those resources without first restoring the neurological infrastructure is not just ineffective. It is a form of institutional gaslighting, offering help in a form the person cannot use and then interpreting their failure to use it as evidence that they do not want help.

The sequential resolution works in two phases, corresponding to two philosophical traditions.

Phase Zero — the Nussbaum Phase: Biological stability is provided unconditionally through clinical environment design. The individual does not need to exercise agency to receive the biological floor. It is provided because human dignity requires it, not because the person has demonstrated the capacity to use it. The clinician designs the environment and ensures the inputs, warmth, nutrition, protected sleep, are continuously available regardless of the person’s current cognitive state. This is not paternalism in the pejorative sense. It is the same logic by which a surgeon stabilizes an unconscious patient before asking them to consent to further treatment. You restore the capacity for choice before asking them to choose. The Nussbaum framework, providing the foundational capabilities of life, bodily health, and bodily integrity as a threshold obligation, governs this phase.

The Dunbar Pod — the Sen Phase: Once biological baselines are restored through the Phase Zero clinical environment, the individual transitions to a self-governed communal environment, participates in pod councils, and regains genuine agency over their daily life. Now the external conditions, community, support, resources, can be used, because the internal capacity has been rebuilt. The Sen framework, the authentic expansion of capabilities, the restoration of freedom to live a life one has reason to value, governs this phase. Housing First principles apply in the Sen Phase: real autonomy, real choice, real democratic participation within the pod community and, eventually, beyond it.

The Internal Conversion Factor

Sen used the concept of a conversion factor to describe the individual’s ability to turn a resource into a capability. The same resource produces different capabilities in different people depending on their conversion factors, their physical condition, their cognitive function, their social situation, their skills and knowledge. A food market in the neighborhood does not produce the same nutritional capability for a person who can walk to it and navigate its aisles and pay for its contents as it does for a person who cannot do any of those things.

MDI restores the conversion factor before providing the resource. The 30-day minimum in the Phase Zero environment is the period in which the biological machinery for converting housing into actual shelter, the capacity for routine, for self-care, for social management, for the sequential execution of daily living tasks, is rebuilt to the point where it can function reliably enough for the transition to the Dunbar Pod to be clinically appropriate.

What follows, in the Dunbar Pod and beyond, is the provision of resources to a person who has regained the conversion capacity to use them. The pod community provides peer support, which the person can now use because their social processing has recovered enough to engage with peers. The clinical services provide mental health support, which the person can now benefit from because their prefrontal function has recovered enough to engage with a therapeutic conversation. The Gardien pathway provides vocational development, which the person can now pursue because their working memory and planning capacity have recovered enough to hold a role and perform its tasks.

The sequence is not incidental. It is the engineering requirement produced by the biology. You cannot run these processes in reverse order and produce good outcomes. You must restore the biological floor first.

The Political Implication

The capability approach has a direct political implication that is worth stating explicitly, because it reframes what a vote for homelessness infrastructure means.

When a voter supports a bond measure for MDI-style stabilization infrastructure, they are not voting for charity. They are not voting for a gesture of compassion. They are voting to fulfill the city’s obligation to provide the foundational capabilities, bodily health, bodily integrity, life, without which democratic participation, civic contribution, and the full exercise of human potential are impossible. They are voting, in other words, for the infrastructure of democracy itself: the physical conditions without which the political system’s claim to represent all its members is a fiction.

That is a different kind of argument than the one typically made for homelessness spending. It is a stronger argument. And it is the one that the capability approach, applied honestly to the conditions of chronic unsheltered homelessness, demands.
