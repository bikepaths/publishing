# Chapter 5: The Human Body as a Physical System

## Chapter 5: The Human Body as a Physical System

Consider a thought experiment. Imagine a city council that decided, for budgetary reasons, to eliminate the water infrastructure. No water treatment plants. No pipes. No delivery systems. Citizens would find water however they could, from rivers, from rain, from neighbors who had stored some. Some would get enough. Many would not. The wealthy would adapt. The poor and the sick would suffer most severely. We would not, looking at the resulting crisis, conclude that the people dying of dehydration had a personal responsibility problem. We would say, without hesitation, that the city had failed at one of its basic functions.

Now substitute sleep, temperature regulation, and nutrition for water. The logic is identical. These are not preferences or amenities. They are biological necessities, the inputs without which the human organism cannot maintain the physical state we call health, and cannot maintain the neurological state we call agency. A city that provides water infrastructure but does not provide sleep infrastructure, temperature infrastructure, and nutrition infrastructure has not fulfilled its basic obligations to its most vulnerable citizens. It has simply chosen which infrastructure failure it considers acceptable.

The Body as a Thermodynamic System

The most useful framework for understanding why the biological floor matters is to think about the human body the way a physicist or engineer would: as an open thermodynamic system. This is not a metaphor. It is a description.

An open thermodynamic system is one that must continuously exchange energy and matter with its environment to maintain its organized internal state. Living things are the clearest examples. A cell, an organ, a body are all highly organized, low-entropy structures existing in a universe that tends toward disorder. Maintaining that organization requires continuous energy input. The body must continuously eat, drink, breathe, and sleep to stay alive. When those inputs are cut off, when the body is prevented from importing sufficient energy and expelling sufficient waste, the organized structure breaks down. In technical terms, entropy increases. In practical terms, the body decays.

The street cuts off the inputs the body requires. Extreme temperatures force the body to divert metabolic energy to thermoregulation, the work of keeping the core temperature stable, and that energy is then not available for cognitive function, immune response, or tissue repair. Nutritional deprivation reduces the supply of the substrates the brain requires to produce neurotransmitters and maintain neural tissue. Sleep deprivation prevents the metabolic waste clearance and synaptic repair that the brain performs during rest. Each of these deprivations compounds the others, and the combined effect is exactly what the thermodynamic model predicts: progressive structural disorganization.

The Biological Floor: Specific Numbers

The MDI model does not treat the biological floor as a philosophical concept. It treats it as a set of engineering specifications derived from clinical physiology. The numbers are not arbitrary. They represent the median requirements for human homeostasis, the physiological conditions under which the body can maintain its organized state and the brain can maintain its functional capacity.

Temperature: the body minimizes the metabolic work of thermoregulation within an ambient temperature range of approximately 65 to 75 degrees Fahrenheit. Outside this range, and especially below 55 degrees where the risk of hypothermia begins to increase, the body must burn energy to maintain core temperature, diverting resources from cognitive maintenance.

Nutrition: a median adult body requires approximately 2,200 kilocalories and 2.5 liters of clean water daily to maintain cellular function and prevent cachexia, the progressive wasting of muscle and organ tissue that occurs under sustained nutritional deprivation. The caloric number is a median and will vary by body size, age, and activity level, but it represents the baseline below which physical deterioration is certain.

Sleep: the brain requires a minimum of six to eight hours of continuous, low-decibel, dark sleep for the restorative processes described in Chapter 2 to occur. Matthew Walker’s research on sleep neuroscience, summarized in his 2017 book on sleep and dreams, documents these requirements with precision. The word “continuous” is critical. Fragmented sleep, four hours interrupted by noise and threat, does not produce the same restorative outcome as six hours of uninterrupted rest.

Below the Floor: What Happens

When the body falls below the biological floor on any of these dimensions, the consequences are measurable and predictable. Temperature below the floor: metabolic resources are diverted to thermoregulation, and the brain, which is metabolically very expensive and the first to lose resources when supply drops, reduces its higher functions. Nutrition below the floor: the brain cannot produce adequate neurotransmitters, and cognitive function, planning, memory, emotional regulation, degrades. Sleep below the floor: toxic metabolic byproducts accumulate in neural tissue, cortisol fails to reset, and prefrontal function declines.

The result is a brain that has entered what neuroscientists call a state of chronic hypervigilance. The threat-detection systems, ancient, fast, non-verbal, are running at maximum. The planning and reasoning systems, newer, slower, effortful, are running at minimum or not at all. This is not a personality. It is a neurological state produced by environmental conditions.

Entropy as a Normative Boundary

Here is the philosophical implication that turns a biological fact into a political obligation. A person whose brain is in a state of chronic hypervigilance cannot exercise democratic rights. They cannot evaluate political candidates, understand ballot measures, fulfill civic responsibilities, or participate meaningfully in the social contract. Crossing the homeostatic threshold does not merely harm the individual; it destroys their capacity to exist as a civic agent.

If a city claims to value human life, democratic participation, and the rule of law, it is logically required to provide the infrastructure that makes those things possible. Not as charity. Not as compassion. As the physical prerequisite for the values the city claims to hold.

The biological floor is not a luxury standard. It is the precondition for everything else that a democratic society considers important.
