## Chapter 24: Riparian Encampments and Environmental Law

The Los Angeles River is 51 miles long. For most of its length, it runs through a concrete channel, engineered in the 1930s and 1940s to prevent the catastrophic flooding that had repeatedly inundated the Los Angeles basin. But at certain points along its course, particularly in the reaches that pass through Elysian Valley, the Sepulveda Basin, and the lower river near Long Beach, the river retains its natural character: willow woodland, mulefat scrub, native reeds, and the associated wildlife that depends on riparian habitat in an otherwise densely urbanized landscape.

These natural reaches are federally protected under the Clean Water Act and the Migratory Bird Treaty Act. They are home to bird species that migrate through the Pacific Flyway, to native fish species that have been reintroduced through decades of conservation effort, and to a range of invertebrates and plant communities that exist nowhere else in the urban core. They are also home to hundreds of people who have built encampments in the vegetation along the river banks.

This is the dual crisis that the C-Riparian pipeline category is designed to address.

The Environmental Obligation

The Clean Water Act requires municipalities to eliminate documented sources of water quality violations in jurisdictional waterways. Encampments along the Los Angeles River generate multiple categories of water quality violations: human waste and associated pathogens enter the groundwater and the river through direct deposit and stormwater flow. Pharmaceutical residue from discarded medications and drug use contaminate the water column. Decomposing organic material from abandoned food waste increases biological oxygen demand in the river, stressing aquatic life. Improper disposal of batteries, electronics, and other materials introduces heavy metals and synthetic chemicals.

These are not hypothetical harms. They have been documented by water quality monitoring, and the municipality has legal obligations to address them regardless of how the contamination is being generated. The presence of people in the encampments does not excuse the municipality from its Clean Water Act obligations. But it absolutely determines how those obligations must be fulfilled.

The Sequencing Requirement

The MDI model is explicit and non-negotiable on this point: human clinical extraction must precede environmental remediation in every case. Clean Water Act restoration protocols are placed on hold until ACT field teams have completed the extraction process.

This sequencing is required on both ethical and practical grounds. Ethically: you do not send remediation equipment and crews into an area where people are living, because displacement without a clinical alternative simply moves the problem without solving it and inflicts additional trauma on people who are already in crisis. Practically: environmental remediation that occurs while people are still in the area will need to be repeated, because the source of contamination has not been removed.

The sequencing requirement demands close operational coordination between human services and environmental agencies, coordination that does not naturally occur because these agencies have different mandates, different budget cycles, and different institutional cultures. Creating the operational framework for that coordination is one of the primary challenges of C-Riparian response.

Hazard Assessment and Remediation Levels

After the human extraction is complete, the environmental response is calibrated to the hazard level of the site. The hazard assessment examines the degree of biological contamination, the presence and concentration of chemical contaminants, the proximity to sensitive habitat, and the extent of damage to native vegetation.

Sites with hazard levels above a defined threshold, severe contamination or proximity to particularly sensitive habitat, trigger dispatch of the EPA Brownfield team, which has specialized expertise and equipment for remediating heavily contaminated sites. Below the high-hazard threshold, standard Clean Water Act restoration protocols apply: removal of accumulated waste and debris, soil remediation if necessary, revegetation with native species, and water quality monitoring to verify improvement.

The revegetation work is an opportunity as well as an obligation. The native plant communities of the Los Angeles River riparian corridor, willows, cottonwoods, mulefat, native sedges and rushes, can be restored through deliberate planting using locally sourced native plant material. Restored riparian habitat provides ecological services that benefit the entire urban watershed, and its restoration is a genuine long-term contribution to the environmental quality of the city.

After Extraction: Category Dissolution

As established in Chapter 11, once an individual is extracted from the wetlands and provided initial stabilization, the C-Riparian designation dissolves. The individual is then classified in the standard pipeline framework. If anosognosia is present and the individual meets C-Calcified criteria, they enter that pathway. If not, they enter the B-Engageable pathway.

The C-Riparian tag was never a description of who they are. It was a description of where they were and what the operational response to their location required. After extraction, their path forward is determined by their clinical state, not by where they were living.
