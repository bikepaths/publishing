# Chapter 22: The Institutional Waste Argument

## Chapter 22: The Institutional Waste Argument

In 2023, a UCLA study examining Los Angeles County’s homelessness spending found that the average cost to the system per chronically unsheltered individual per year, counting emergency room visits, ambulance runs, police responses, incarceration, psychiatric emergency services, and the administrative costs of programs that do not produce durable housing, exceeded $100,000. The average cost of a fully supported MDI residential slot, including clinical services, nutrition, maintenance, and overhead, was projected at approximately $40,000 to $60,000 per year.

The reactive system costs more than the preventative system. It produces worse outcomes. And it continues to be funded, year after year, because the institutional and political structures that maintain it have strong interests in their own continuation.

This is the institutional waste argument, and it is both the strongest fiscal case for MDI and the most significant explanation for why MDI has not been built.

The 85 Percent Waste Index

The 85 percent waste index, the figure representing the proportion of reactive municipal spending that produces no durable reduction in the unsheltered population, is derived from analysis of California State Auditor findings and HUD Office of Inspector General efficiency analyses. It is an illustrative parameter, not a precisely calculated number. But the direction of the finding is not in serious dispute: the vast majority of current municipal homelessness spending is reactive management of symptoms, not investment in infrastructure that addresses causes.

Reactive spending includes: emergency room visits for conditions that preventative stabilization would have avoided; ambulance transports triggered by crises that adequate shelter would have prevented; police responses to encampment complaints and survival behavior violations; incarceration for non-violent survival behaviors; psychiatric emergency services for crises that clinical stabilization would have intercepted; and the enormous administrative apparatus of programs that enroll people, provide temporary services, and then lose them back to the street without producing durable change.

Each of these expenditures is a response to a real crisis in the moment it occurs. The problem is not that the responders are wrong to respond. The problem is that the system is structured to produce the crises rather than to prevent them. Every dollar spent on an ambulance run that could have been prevented by a stabilization environment is a dollar that did not build the stabilization environment. The reactive system is self-perpetuating.

Institutional Capture

Why does the reactive system persist despite its failure? The answer is not primarily ignorance. Most people working in the homelessness system understand its limitations. The answer is institutional capture: the structural dynamic in which organizations that are built around the current model have budget allocations, staff positions, contractual relationships, and political constituencies that depend on the continuation of that model.

A city agency whose budget depends on administering emergency shelter contracts has an institutional interest in the continuation of emergency shelter as the primary response to homelessness. This is not malice. It is the ordinary behavior of organizations in competitive resource environments. Organizations protect their budgets. Programs protect their contracts. Staff protect their positions. None of these behaviors requires bad intent, and none of them is the result of policy analysis. They are the structural outputs of institutional self-preservation in a resource-constrained environment.

Acknowledging this is not cynicism. It is a prerequisite for designing around it. A reform proposal that requires existing institutions to voluntarily reduce their own budgets will fail, because institutions do not voluntarily reduce their own budgets. A reform proposal that creates new institutions with new budget streams, that demonstrates superior outcomes through rigorous measurement, and that builds political constituencies around those outcomes has a chance of succeeding.

The Outcomes Audit Requirement

The solution to institutional waste is not simply spending more money or spending less money. It is changing what the money is measured against. Currently, most homelessness programs are accountable for administrative throughput: how many people were served, how many beds were filled, how many appointments were kept. These are process measures. They tell you whether the program is operating. They do not tell you whether the program is working.

MDI’s effectiveness depends on measuring genuine biological and civic outcomes: rates of prefrontal recovery as measured by validated cognitive assessments, rates of stable housing retention at 6 and 12 months, rates of emergency room utilization before and after MDI enrollment, rates of psychiatric hospitalization before and after enrollment, rates of municipal criminal justice contact before and after enrollment. These are outcome measures. They tell you whether the program is working.

The social impact bond mechanism described in Chapter 21 is one mechanism for creating financial accountability to outcomes: investors only get paid if verified outcomes are achieved, which creates a financial incentive for the program to prioritize genuine outcomes over administrative throughput. But the outcomes audit requirement is broader than the bond mechanism. It applies to all MDI programming: every expenditure should be traceable to a clinical outcome, and clinical outcomes should be regularly and independently verified.
