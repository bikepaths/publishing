## Chapter 15: Metabolic Stabilization

Thirty days is the minimum. Not because thirty days is convenient for budgeting cycles or discharge planning timelines. Because thirty days is approximately the minimum duration of sustained adequate sleep, thermal stability, and nutritional sufficiency required for measurable early recovery of prefrontal cortex function to begin.

This timeline is derived from neuroscience. The neurological changes produced by chronic street exposure, the dendritic retraction, the reduction in neurogenesis, the dysregulation of the HPA axis, do not reverse quickly. They reverse slowly, given the right conditions, over a timeline measured in weeks and months. Trying to compress the timeline produces predictable failure: people are discharged into housing before their brains have recovered enough to maintain that housing, and they cycle back to the street.

The 30-day minimum is a neurobiological fact, not an administrative preference.

The Five Clearance Indicators

Biological clearance from the Phase Zero Metabolic Stabilization Unit is determined by five measurable indicators:

First, sustained hygiene maintenance, the person is consistently maintaining basic personal hygiene without prompting or external support. This is a proxy for executive function and for sleep and nutritional recovery.

Second, sleep continuity, the person is consistently achieving six or more hours of uninterrupted sleep. This is both a prerequisite for recovery and an indicator of it: a brain that has begun recovering from chronic hypervigilance will naturally achieve deeper and more continuous sleep.

Third, consistent orientation to time and place, the person reliably knows where they are, what day it is, and what is happening in their environment. This is a basic indicator of cognitive stability.

Fourth, group participation, the person is engaging with other people in the facility in some minimal way: sharing a meal, participating in a conversation, acknowledging and responding to others. This is not sociability in any broad sense; it is the minimum indicator that the brain’s social processing is functioning.

Fifth, independent activities of daily living, the person is performing basic self-maintenance tasks independently: feeding themselves, managing their own space, managing their own schedule to a minimal degree.

The 40 Percent Threshold

The clearance threshold is not all five indicators. It is 40 percent of active indicators, meaning that if all five are being measured, the person needs to meet at least two of them consistently to trigger a clearance review.

This threshold is explicitly provisional. It is a starting parameter, derived from clinical judgment in the absence of longitudinal data from MDI installations. It is not a clinically validated standard. The intent is that it serves as the initial clearance parameter to be calibrated over time by data from actual MDI deployments, as the system accumulates outcome data, the threshold can be refined to reflect what actually predicts successful pod transition.

The 40 percent threshold reflects a clinical judgment that the system should err on the side of moving people toward recovery conditions rather than holding them in the more intensive Phase Zero environment past the point of biological necessity. It is a conservative estimate, not a final determination.

The Telemetry Requirement

The clearance assessment cannot use stale data. The MDI system requires that all data used in clearance decisions be no older than 24 hours. If the most recent data on any clearance indicator is older than 24 hours, the system raises an error and requires a human clinical override before any clearance decision proceeds.

This requirement exists because the clinical state of someone in early recovery can change significantly over 24 hours. A clearance decision made on data from three days ago may not accurately reflect the person’s current state. In a system where automated tools support clinical decision-making, the data freshness requirement is a critical safeguard against automation error.

What Clearance Is and Is Not

Biological clearance from Phase Zero does not mean the person is well. It means that their biological state has stabilized sufficiently that the transition from the intensive Phase Zero environment to the more autonomous Dunbar Pod environment is clinically appropriate. Support continues after clearance. The clinical relationship continues. The intensity of the environment changes.

Clearance does not require sobriety. It does not require a psychiatric diagnosis to be resolved. It does not require behavioral compliance in any conventional sense. It does not require vocational readiness or housing readiness. It requires the biological indicators described above, nothing more.

This distinction is not incidental. It reflects the core design principle of the MDI system: that the standard for progression through the system is biological, not behavioral. Behavioral standards produce the institutional attrition described in Chapter 3. Biological standards produce clinical outcomes.
