# Chapter 18: Upward Mobility

## Chapter 18: Upward Mobility

The goal of the MDI system is not to keep people in the tower. The goal is to restore the capacity for genuine choice about where and how to live. For some people, that means remaining in the Dunbar Pod community for the long term. For others, it means transition to scattered-site independent housing. For others, it means connection to employment and a conventional housing market. All of these are successes, because all of them represent genuine agency over one’s own life.

The distinction matters because systems can be designed, intentionally or not, to produce specific outcomes regardless of what individuals actually want. A system designed around transitioning people to independent housing as quickly as possible will discharge people before they are ready, because the metric of success is discharge, not wellbeing. A system designed around maintaining people in supported housing indefinitely will create dependency, because the metric of success is retention, not growth. MDI is designed around genuine agency, which means the outcome is whatever the individual, with restored cognitive capacity, actually chooses.

The Upward Mobility Pathway

The pipeline categories are not permanent stations. They are descriptions of current clinical state with defined transition criteria. The transitions move in one direction: toward greater autonomy and less clinical intensity.

C-Riparian transitions to C-Calcified or B-Engageable after successful wetlands extraction, depending on anosognosia status.

C-Calcified transitions to B-Engageable after approximately 12 months of stabilization and resolution of anosognosia. This is the longest and most clinically intensive transition, and it is not guaranteed on any specific timeline. The 12-month figure is a general parameter, not a hard discharge date.

B-Engageable transitions to A-Voluntary when economic readiness is established, when the individual has developed the internal conversion capacity to utilize standard housing resources.

A-Voluntary transitions to the broader coordinated entry system and standard housing market services.

Economic Readiness

Economic readiness is a specific concept in the MDI model that is worth defining precisely. It does not mean full employment. It does not mean financial independence. It does not mean the absence of ongoing challenges. It means the internal conversion capacity, the cognitive function, social connection, and practical skills, to navigate the mainstream housing market and the employment system with the level of support that those systems normally provide.

A person who has spent a year in a Dunbar Pod, who has relationships with neighbors and staff, who has a regular daily rhythm, who has basic documentation in order, and who has participated in the Gardien pipeline to any degree has substantially more economic readiness than they arrived with. The question at the point of transition is not whether they are fully functional by mainstream standards. It is whether they have enough functioning capacity to be supported by mainstream systems, rather than requiring the more intensive support of the MDI environment.

The Gardien Pipeline as an Upward Mobility Engine

The Gardien vocational pathway, described in Chapter 13, is the primary mechanism within the MDI system for building economic readiness. Peer Support Specialist certification, achieved after twelve months of housing stability and 180 conflict-free days with demonstrated leadership aptitude, is a credentialed professional qualification recognized by the California Department of Health Care Services and equivalent bodies in other states. A Certified Peer Support Specialist can work in community mental health agencies, hospital discharge planning programs, housing case management, and a range of other settings.

This is not a made-up credential. It pays real wages. It is a real pathway from MDI resident to employed member of the community mental health workforce, a workforce that, across the country, is severely understaffed and actively seeking people with lived experience of homelessness and psychiatric illness, because those individuals are uniquely credible as peer support providers for the populations those agencies serve.

Population-Level Success

At the population level, success in the MDI system does not look like an empty tower. It looks like a flowing pipeline: people entering through Phase Zero, stabilizing, transitioning to Dunbar Pods, developing economic readiness, and moving on to independent housing, with the tower continuously receiving new arrivals from the street who begin the same process.

A tower that never empties is not a failure. It is evidence that the infrastructure is being used for its purpose: providing ongoing stabilization for the population that needs it, producing transitions for those ready to move on, and maintaining community for those for whom the Dunbar Pod is the right long-term environment. The measure of success is not bed count. It is the rate at which people are making genuine progress toward the form of life they choose.
