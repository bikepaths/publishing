# Chapter 27: What Citizens Can Do

## Chapter 27: What Citizens Can Do

This book has been building toward this question. You have read the science, the engineering, the funding architecture, and the ethics. The problem is clear. The solution is specified. The financing is assemblyable. The political obstacles are identifiable. What do you do now?

The answer to that question depends heavily on who you are, where you live, what you do, and where in your life you are. This chapter offers several distinct pathways, because the work of implementing MDI will require people with very different skills and positions to contribute in very different ways.

If You Vote

You already have the most basic lever. Pay attention to what candidates for city council, county supervisor, mayor, and state legislature say about homelessness, and hold them accountable for whether their positions reflect an understanding of the neuroscience, the economics, and the engineering described in this book, or whether they are reciting familiar bromides about personal responsibility and law enforcement that have not worked for 50 years.

When a ballot measure proposes funding for homelessness programs, learn enough about where the money is going to know whether it is investing in preventative infrastructure or reactive management. The difference between a bond measure that funds MDI-style stabilization infrastructure and a bond measure that funds another round of emergency shelter contracts is the difference between an investment and an expense.

Attend a zoning meeting when supportive housing is proposed in your neighborhood or city. Attend not to oppose it but to understand what is being proposed, to ask informed questions about the management model and the clinical infrastructure, and to provide the voice of a neighbor who understands why this infrastructure is necessary and supports it being well-built and well-managed.

If You Are in a Relevant Profession

Urban planning, public health, Medicaid administration, clinical social work, real estate development, municipal finance, environmental law: all of these professions have a direct role in MDI implementation. If you work in any of these fields, the MDI model is your domain.

Urban planners can advocate within their departments and professional organizations for utility classification frameworks that protect essential health infrastructure from NIMBY veto. Public health professionals can build the evidence base for MDI by designing and implementing the outcome measurement systems that will demonstrate its effectiveness and justify continued investment. Medicaid administrators can work toward 1115 Waiver structures in their states that unlock Medicaid revenue for housing-adjacent services. Clinical social workers can work toward the ACT team models and peer support specialist credentialing frameworks that supply the MDI clinical workforce. Real estate developers can identify distressed commercial buildings in their markets and evaluate conversion feasibility.

None of this requires waiting for a policy decision from above. Much of it can begin within existing institutional frameworks as incremental change that builds the conditions for larger transformation.

Open Source Tools for Analysis

The MDI model is built on a commitment to open tools and open data. The analysis required to advocate for MDI in your city, mapping encampment locations, identifying distressed commercial real estate, analyzing municipal homelessness spending, tracking health outcomes, can be done with freely available tools.

QGIS is an open source geographic information system that allows you to map data spatially, overlaying encampment location data with commercial vacancy data, transit access, proximity to existing service infrastructure, and any other spatial data relevant to MDI siting analysis. It is freely downloadable and well-documented.

Python, with libraries including pandas for data analysis, geopandas for spatial analysis, and matplotlib for visualization, provides the analytical tools for working with the datasets that federal agencies publish: HUD point-in-time count data, CoC program performance data, FHA housing cost data, Census Bureau economic data. All of this data is publicly available and can be downloaded and analyzed without any specialized software license.

OpenStreetMap provides freely available geographic data for any city in the world, street networks, building footprints, point-of-interest data, that can be used in QGIS analyses without any licensing cost.

The HUD data portal publishes annual point-in-time counts for every Continuum of Care in the country, along with CoC program spending and housing inventory data that allows you to analyze the pattern of homelessness and housing investment in your own city.

Using these tools, a knowledgeable citizen, or a student, or a researcher, or a journalist, can build the kind of locally specific analysis that makes the MDI argument concrete rather than abstract: which specific buildings in your city are the best candidates for conversion, what the current pattern of reactive spending looks like and what it could fund if redirected, how the encampment population in your city relates to the existing service infrastructure.

How to Read a City Budget

Municipal budgets are public documents in every American city. They are long, technical, and deliberately difficult to navigate. But the information you need is in them.

Look for the homelessness-related line items under the city attorney’s office (encampment enforcement, litigation), the police department (response to encampment complaints), the fire department (ambulance runs to encampment-related calls), the public health department (emergency health services), and whatever department administers homelessness programs directly. Add those numbers up. Compare the total to the amount spent on housing development and stabilization infrastructure. The ratio will tell you what the city actually prioritizes, as opposed to what it says it prioritizes.

Look at the program descriptions for homelessness funding. Are they funding operations, staff, services, or capital investment? Operations funding produces recurring annual costs. Capital investment produces assets that generate returns. A city that spends all its homelessness budget on operations and nothing on capital infrastructure is renting services rather than building the infrastructure of recovery.

The Open Questions

This book has argued for the MDI model with as much confidence as the available evidence supports. That confidence is genuine. But intellectual honesty requires acknowledging what we do not yet know.

The specific clearance indicators described in Chapter 15 and the 40 percent threshold are provisional parameters, not clinically validated standards. Longitudinal studies of people in MDI-style installations, tracking biological markers, cognitive function, housing retention, and civic participation over time, are necessary to validate and refine those parameters. That research has not yet been done at the scale required to be definitive.

The specific weights in the Structural Fitness Index, the algorithm that determines pod assignment, are similarly provisional. Machine learning calibration based on outcomes data from actual MDI deployments is required to refine those weights. The model as specified is a reasonable starting point. It is not a final answer.

The legal instrument landscape outside California is incompletely mapped. Chapter 16 describes California’s specific legal tools and notes that national equivalents exist, but the specific criteria, triggering mechanisms, and judicial oversight structures in other states vary and in some states are considerably less developed than California’s. Building MDI outside California requires legal mapping work that this book has not done.

These open questions are not reasons to delay. They are the research agenda for the next generation of urban scholars, clinical researchers, and policy analysts who will refine the model as it is implemented. The generational investment this book represents is not a demand for perfection before action. It is an argument for beginning, and for building the research infrastructure that will improve the model over time.

The Generational Timeline

Charles DiBella does not expect MDI to be implemented in his lifetime. This is not pessimism. It is an accurate reading of how major urban infrastructure transformations happen. Sanitation systems were theorized in the mid-nineteenth century and not universally implemented until the early twentieth. Public transit networks were theorized and debated for decades before they were built. The transformation of American cities through the highway system was planned for twenty years before construction began.

The transformation required to end chronic homelessness is of comparable scale. It requires changes in how cities budget, how state Medicaid programs are structured, how federal tax credits are allocated, how zoning law treats public health infrastructure, and how the political system weighs the interests of the visible, organized, housed constituency against the interests of the invisible, unorganized, unsheltered one.

None of these changes happens quickly. All of them are possible. The history of urban infrastructure is the history of impossible ideas becoming obvious ones, of problems that seemed intractable until the political conditions, the financing mechanisms, and the public will aligned to make them tractable.

This book is a contribution to that alignment, an attempt to put the right framework into enough hands that when the political conditions mature, the intellectual tools are ready. If you finish this book and it changes how you think about cities, and how you vote, and what you demand from the people who represent you, it has done its work.

The rest is up to you.
