# Chapter 3: Why Current Systems Fail

## Chapter 3: Why Current Systems Fail

Follow Maria through a Tuesday in Los Angeles. Maria is 47 years old. She has been on the street for three years, following an eviction she could not prevent after her employer reduced her hours below the threshold for health insurance, and a medical bill she could not pay destroyed her credit. She is not experiencing psychosis. She does not have a substance use disorder. She is, by any reasonable standard, one of the easier cases, someone whose needs are primarily economic and whose cognitive function, though degraded by three years of street exposure, is still largely intact.

Her Tuesday begins at 4:30 AM, when the encampment where she sleeps becomes too dangerous to remain in after a fight breaks out nearby. She moves to a doorway two blocks away. At 6:00 AM, she is told to move by a building security guard. She needs to be at the Coordinated Entry System office by 8:30 AM for an appointment she has been trying to keep for six weeks, the last two attempts having failed when her phone, which she charges at a library, died before she received the confirmation text. She has her identification documents, a victory, because she lost her birth certificate twice and each replacement requires a trip to a different office with proof of identity she does not have. She arrives at 8:45, fifteen minutes late. She is told her appointment has been given to someone else and she must reschedule.

This is not a story about incompetent caseworkers or an underfunded office. It is a story about structural design. A system that requires the most cognitively stressed, logistically challenged, resource-depleted people to navigate complex bureaucratic processes without the cognitive support, reliable communication infrastructure, or physical stability to do so reliably will produce exactly this outcome, every time.

The Behavioral Model and Its Fatal Assumption

The dominant model underlying virtually every current homelessness intervention is what we can call the behavioral model. It assumes that homeless individuals possess intact cognitive agency, the ability to understand instructions, keep appointments, manage documents, and make decisions oriented toward future outcomes, and that what is needed is a system to guide, incentivize, or require them to use that agency in constructive ways.

This assumption was always imperfect. For the population experiencing chronic homelessness, specifically those who have spent years on the street and whose brains have been degraded by that exposure, the assumption is simply false. The behavioral model is trying to elicit outputs from machinery that has been damaged. It will fail, not occasionally and not for solvable logistical reasons, but systematically and predictably.

Every requirement in the standard service system reflects this false assumption. Appointment scheduling assumes the ability to track time reliably, which requires consistent sleep, consistent orientation, and a reliable phone or calendar. Documentation requirements assume the ability to safely store and access papers in an environment where possession theft is endemic. Sobriety requirements assume the ability to make and maintain choices that require prefrontal executive function, precisely the cognitive capacity that chronic street exposure degrades. Compliance milestones assume the ability to perform a reliable sequence of actions over time, which requires planning capacity that is among the first things street exposure destroys.

What Housing First Gets Right, and Where It Falls Short

The Housing First model, developed by Sam Tsemberis in the 1990s and now the dominant paradigm in American homelessness policy, deserves genuine respect for what it got right. Housing First rejects the idea that people must earn housing through demonstrated sobriety or treatment compliance. It provides immediate access to permanent housing without preconditions. It has strong evidence for housing retention: people who receive Housing First services stay housed at significantly higher rates than people who go through traditional staircase programs.

For a significant portion of the homeless population, people experiencing economic displacement, people with functional enough cognitive capacity to manage independent apartment living, people with intact social support networks, Housing First works. It is the right intervention for that population.

The problem is that it does not work for the most severely neurologically damaged population. Placing a person whose prefrontal cortex has been structurally altered by years of street exposure into a scattered-site apartment, alone, in a neighborhood where they know no one, without the environmental supports required for neurological recovery, does not produce the outcomes Housing First achieves with less severely affected populations. The most severely affected people cycle back to the street, not because they don’t want housing, but because independent apartment living requires the cognitive and social capacities that their brains no longer reliably possess.

Housing First was designed for a population that can use housing. For the population that cannot yet use housing, because the street has damaged the biological machinery required to do so, a prior phase of stabilization is necessary. MDI is that phase.

Administrative Attrition and Rational Distrust

There is another mechanism of system failure that deserves naming: institutional attrition driven by rational distrust. People who have experienced the social service system for years have typically experienced it as a source of conditional support that can be withdrawn without warning, of forced displacement, of promises made and not kept, of arrest for survival behaviors, of systems that express care while systematically failing to provide it.

From a caseworker’s desk, a client who stops showing up looks like someone who gave up, or whose needs are too complex, or who is non-compliant. From the client’s perspective, the decision to stop engaging with a system that has repeatedly failed them is often a rational response to accumulated evidence. The rationality of that distrust is not an obstacle to be overcome by communication strategies. It is information about what the system has actually done, and it can only be addressed by the system actually doing something different.

The 85 Percent Problem

Los Angeles County spent approximately $3.7 billion on homelessness programs in fiscal year 2023-2024. The unsheltered population did not meaningfully decline. A California State Auditor analysis found that approximately 85 percent of reactive municipal spending on homelessness, emergency room visits, ambulance runs, police responses, court appearances, encampment sweeps, and the administrative apparatus of emergency shelter, produces no durable reduction in the unsheltered population. The money is spent. The problem persists. The 85 percent figure is not a critique of individuals working within the system. It is a description of what reactive management, as opposed to preventative infrastructure, produces over time.

This is not a uniquely California phenomenon. Any city that spends the majority of its homelessness budget on managing the immediate symptoms of the crisis, rather than on infrastructure that addresses the underlying biological and housing market failures, will find its money producing the same outcome: continuous expenditure, continuous crisis, no durable reduction.

The institutional waste is not the result of incompetence or corruption. It is the structural output of a system designed around reactive management rather than preventative infrastructure. Ambulances respond to overdoses because there is no stabilization infrastructure to prevent them. Police respond to encampment complaints because there is no facility to offer as an alternative. Emergency rooms treat hypothermia and infected wounds because there is no place designed to prevent those conditions from developing. Each individual expenditure is a response to a real crisis. Together, they add up to an enormous investment in managing a problem that nothing in the system is designed to solve.

The Design Conclusion

The current system fails not because of insufficient compassion, insufficient funding, or insufficient effort from the people working within it. It fails because of a structural design error at its foundation: it demands behavioral outputs from people whose neurobiology has been damaged beyond the capacity to produce those outputs reliably, in the quantity and on the timelines the system requires.

Fixing the system does not mean doing more of the same thing with more money. It means designing a different system, one that starts from the biological reality of the population it serves, provides the environmental conditions required for neurological recovery as its first and non-negotiable step, and builds every subsequent intervention on that biological foundation.

That is what the MDI model does.
