## Chapter 14: Phase Zero — The Unconditional Entry

The first principle of the MDI system is also its most radical departure from every existing model: no one is turned away.

Not for intoxication. Not for active psychiatric symptoms. Not for an outstanding warrant. Not for behavioral history. Not for documentation status. The biological floor, hygiene, nutrition, warmth, rest, is provided unconditionally, because the biological floor is what makes everything else possible. A door that closes on the people who need it most is not a service system. It is a selection mechanism that ensures the most damaged people remain the most damaged.

The Needs Assessment

Phase Zero begins not with a gate, but with a needs assessment. The assessment measures what a person needs, how long since their last shower, their nutritional status, their sleep deprivation level, their current substance use status. It does not measure whether they qualify. There is no qualifying threshold. The assessment determines what the immediate intervention should be, not whether the person is entitled to one.

The unconditional baseline is provided to everyone before any further assessment occurs: hygiene access and nutritional support. These are not contingent on any finding of the assessment. They are provided because the body requires them, and the system’s first obligation is to the body.

Triage Logic

After the immediate unconditional baseline is provided, the triage logic determines the appropriate next step. The logic has a defined priority order:

If the person is experiencing an acute medical crisis or overdose, they go directly to the emergency room, immediately, regardless of any other factor, including an active warrant. Medical emergency supersedes everything.

If the person has an active warrant or is in imminent involvement in criminal activity, legal processing occurs, but only after biological stabilization is confirmed. The system does not hand someone to law enforcement while they are in active physiological crisis.

If the person is Pipeline D-Nomadic, they are directed to the Ground Floor Harm Reduction commons, where harm reduction services are available without any pressure to enter the clinical system.

All others are directed to the Metabolic Stabilization Unit.

The Three Ps

One of the most important operational features of Phase Zero is the Three Ps protocol: the systematic processing of the things that most commonly prevent people from accepting shelter, Pets, Possessions, and Partners.

For each: Pets are either approved for in-unit cohabitation if the facility has capacity and the animal’s behavior permits, or placed in a veterinary boarding arrangement managed by the facility, at no cost to the resident. Possessions are transported to a secure storage facility accessible to the resident. Partners are co-located in the same facility, wherever possible in the same pod.

These three accommodations are not amenities. They are retention factors. Research on shelter refusal consistently identifies pets, possessions, and partners as the three most common reasons people who need shelter decline available shelter. The Three Ps protocol eliminates those barriers at the door. A person who enters Phase Zero does not have to choose between their dog and their safety, between their belongings and a bed, or between their partner and a roof. The system accommodates all three.

The 5150 Hold

When a person presents at Phase Zero in a state of acute psychiatric crisis, expressing intent to harm themselves or others, or exhibiting psychosis severe enough to prevent communication, the 5150 hold process may be invoked. Named after the section of the California Welfare and Institutions Code that governs it, a 5150 hold allows a qualified mental health professional to authorize up to 72 hours of involuntary psychiatric evaluation and treatment in a hospital or designated facility.

The 5150 process is not initiated by the system alone, and it is not initiated casually. A qualified mental health professional must make an independent clinical assessment of imminent danger. The hold lasts a maximum of 72 hours at its first stage, after which either the person is released or a higher level of legal authority is sought. The hold is a clinical tool for managing situations of genuine imminent danger.

The cultural assessment requirement applies as a hard prerequisite to any classification that occurs after the immediate unconditional baseline is provided. The system will not assign a pipeline category, initiate compelled care proceedings, or make any clinical determination beyond the immediate needs assessment until the cultural and linguistic baseline is complete. This is not an administrative formality. It is a clinical requirement. An incorrect classification, made without understanding who a person is and where they come from, produces an incorrect intervention. An incorrect intervention in a vulnerable population causes harm.
