## Chapter 17: Re-entry and Cycling

Consider how medicine treats cancer. A patient in remission who experiences a recurrence does not lose their oncologist. They do not get removed from the medical system for failing to maintain remission. They do not have to start over from the beginning of treatment and re-prove their eligibility for care. They receive additional treatment calibrated to their current state, and the information about their recurrence is used to refine the treatment plan going forward.

This is how every serious medical field treats the cyclic nature of serious illness. Cardiology. Addiction medicine. Psychiatric care. Recovery from serious conditions involves cycles. Relapse is not failure. It is a clinical event that contains information about what additional support is needed.

Homelessness policy is one of the few fields in which a return to street conditions is treated as a system failure, as evidence that the person was not ready for housing, or that the intervention did not work, or that resources were wasted. This framing is not only incorrect. It is clinically counterproductive, because it produces systems that punish cycling rather than learning from it.

The Re-Entry Principle

The MDI model operationalizes a different principle: leaving the tower is a recognized clinical cycle, not a failure and not a violation. The system does not discharge a resident. The system maintains a relationship with them, provides harm reduction services if they return to the street, and treats re-entry into the facility as the expected and appropriate next step in a cyclic process.

The 7-day return window recognizes that someone who leaves the tower and returns within 7 days is not in a significantly different clinical state from the one they were in when they left. Their Phase Zero data is still active. Their pod relationships are still current. Their cultural assessment is still valid. These individuals re-enter the Metabolic Stabilization Unit directly, bypassing Phase Zero triage. They return to the system they were already part of.

Someone who returns after more than 7 days restarts standard triage. This is a clinical necessity, not a punishment. Their biological state may have changed significantly during an extended absence. Fresh assessment is required to determine the appropriate next step.

What Cycling Data Tells Us

A person who cycles twice in six months needs a different support configuration than a person who cycles once in three years. The pattern of cycling is information. It tells clinicians about the triggers for re-exit, about the social or environmental conditions that produced the return to the street, about the gap between what the pod environment provided and what this specific individual needed.

The MDI system is designed to learn from cycling. Each cycle generates data that refines the match between the individual and their environment, their pod composition, their cohesion anchor relationships, their clinical support intensity. A system that treats cycling as failure discards this information. A system that treats cycling as a clinical event captures and uses it.

Long-Term Residents as Community Assets

The MDI model explicitly values long-term residents not just as individuals in recovery but as community assets. A person who has been in a Dunbar Pod for three years is not an outlier or a failure case. They are a cohesion anchor, someone whose long history in the community, whose relationships with other residents, and whose embodied knowledge of how the pod works is of clinical value to the newest and most fragile arrivals.

Long-term residents are not required to leave in order to make room for new arrivals. The tower is not a transitional facility with a maximum stay. For some people, the Dunbar Pod community will be the right long-term living arrangement, and that is a successful outcome. For others, the pod is a transitional environment on the way to more independent housing. Both outcomes are successes, and neither should be privileged over the other by the system’s design.
