## Chapter 21: Private Capital and Blended Finance

The private sector has a financial interest in solving chronic homelessness. This may sound counterintuitive; the private sector is often positioned in homelessness debates as the antagonist, the force of gentrification and real estate speculation that contributes to housing unaffordability. That characterization has merit in many contexts. But when it comes to funding the solution to chronic homelessness, the private sector’s financial interests align strongly with the public interest, and that alignment is the financial foundation for the private participation that MDI requires.

The alignment works through a simple mechanism: chronic homelessness is very expensive for private institutions, and reducing it would reduce those costs.

Hospital Systems

Major hospital systems in cities with large unsheltered populations bear enormous costs in uncompensated emergency care. A chronically unsheltered person experiencing a psychiatric crisis goes to an emergency room. A person with an untreated infection goes to an emergency room. A person experiencing hypothermia goes to an emergency room. These visits cost, on average, thousands of dollars each, costs that are not recovered from uninsured patients and are only partially recovered through Medicaid. A major hospital in downtown Los Angeles may be spending tens of millions of dollars annually in uncompensated emergency care for the chronically unsheltered population.

A hospital that contributes capital to an MDI tower within its service area is not making a charitable donation. It is making a strategic investment that reduces its own emergency department costs by reducing the number of preventable crises in the surrounding population. The math is straightforward: if an MDI tower prevents a fraction of the emergency room visits that its residents would otherwise generate, and if each prevented visit saves the hospital thousands of dollars, the hospital’s cost savings over five to ten years can exceed its initial capital contribution.

The MDI model formalizes this as a Hospital Capital Contribution: upfront equity invested by healthcare systems in exchange for dedicated discharge beds in the tower, beds to which the hospital can discharge patients who need a level of support between acute inpatient care and independent living, avoiding the problem of discharging complex patients to the street for lack of an appropriate alternative.

Social Impact Bonds

A social impact bond is a financing instrument in which private investors provide upfront capital for a social program, and receive financial returns, paid by the government, if and only if the program achieves verified outcomes. The risk of program failure is borne by the private investors: if the outcomes are not achieved, the investors do not get paid. If the outcomes are achieved, the investors receive returns funded by the government savings those outcomes produce.

For MDI, the social impact bond structure works like this: investors fund the upfront capital costs of a tower. The outcomes measured are the documented reductions in municipal costs, emergency room visits, ambulance runs, incarceration, psychiatric hospitalization, generated by stable housing of the tower’s residents. An independent auditor verifies the outcomes. The government pays returns to investors from the verified savings.

This structure is attractive to a specific class of investors, impact investors and mission-driven institutional funds, because it provides financial return alongside documented social impact. It is attractive to municipalities because it shifts the financial risk of program failure to private investors: if the MDI tower does not achieve the expected outcomes, the municipality does not owe the investors their returns.

Community Development Financial Institutions

CDFIs, Community Development Financial Institutions, are mission-driven lenders certified by the federal CDFI Fund that fill the gap between conventional commercial lending and public subsidy. CDFIs are willing to lend at below-market rates to projects that serve their communities, in exchange for the mission alignment that commercial banks may not prioritize.

In an MDI capital stack, a CDFI might provide the debt financing, the loan component of the stack, at an interest rate below what a commercial bank would charge, because the CDFI values the social outcomes of the project. This lower interest rate reduces the annual debt service cost of the project, improving its operational sustainability.

LIHTC equity provides roughly 45 percent of total project cost, generated by the sale of federal tax credits to institutional investors. NMTC allocation provides an additional 15 percent, layered on top of the LIHTC transaction. A social impact bond provides 15 percent as private investor capital secured against projected municipal savings. Hospital capital contributions provide 10 percent in exchange for dedicated discharge beds. A CDFI loan provides 10 percent in below-market debt. Municipal land contribution, the city contributes land or a distressed building at nominal value, provides the remaining 5 percent.

Total project cost for a 150-unit conversion at distressed commercial real estate pricing, approximately one-sixth of ground-up construction cost, per Urban Land Institute estimates for the post-COVID market, is roughly $30 to $40 million, compared to $180 to $240 million for equivalent ground-up construction. The capital stack described above is achievable at that price point.

This is not speculation. The individual financing instruments described above are all in active use. The challenge is assembling them in a single coherent transaction, which requires sophisticated financial structuring, legal coordination, and relationships with the right investors and lenders. That challenge is real. But it is a financial engineering challenge, not a fundamental obstacle.
