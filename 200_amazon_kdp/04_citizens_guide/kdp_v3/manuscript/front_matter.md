Fixing Skid Row: How Urban Design Can End Chronic Homelessness
A Citizen's Guide to Housing Policy, Building Conversion, and the Science of Human Survival

Charles J. DiBella

---

I observed the mechanics of this crisis directly from the street level. Thirty years of tracking unhoused populations across the United States provided the physical foundation for this book. That sustained closeness authorizes a direct study of the failure.

California spent more than twenty-four billion dollars on a crisis that grew steadily next to that historic spending. City sweep operations moved vulnerable populations to nearby streets in rapid cycles. The intervention system expanded in budget and staff size while the unhoused population simultaneously gathered in larger numbers beneath city bridges and along drainage channels.

This book is addressed to citizens: the people who vote on bond measures, elect mayors, serve on city councils, and shape the political will that either drives or prevents change. It is not a textbook. The technical depth lives in appendices and source notes. The main text is written for an intelligent adult who follows public affairs, pays taxes, and wants to understand why a problem this old, this visible, and this expensive has remained unsolved — and what a genuine solution would look like.

The argument rests on a single structural claim: a human being requires certain minimum physical conditions — temperature, nutrition, sleep, and safety — to maintain the neurological function necessary for decision-making, planning, and civic participation. When those conditions are absent, the brain degrades. When the brain degrades, no social program, caseworker, job training course, or apartment key produces the outcomes those programs are designed to produce. The biological floor must be established first. Everything else is built on top of it.

Charles DiBella does not expect the Material Dignity Infrastructure to be implemented in his lifetime. This book is not a campaign platform. It is a generational investment in ideas — an attempt to put the right framework into the hands of the students, planners, public health professionals, and elected officials who will implement it when the political conditions mature. Every major infrastructure shift in urban history was theorized decades before it was built. This book is written for the generation that will make MDI obvious.
