# Chapter 13: Rapid Exit Protocol

The county transit authority dispatch floor occupied the second story of a concrete building on Vignes Street, three blocks east of Union Station. Banks of flat-panel monitors lined the walls, displaying the real-time GPS positions of every bus, rail car, and maintenance vehicle operating across the metropolitan network. The room hummed with the constant murmur of radio chatter and the clicking of dispatchers typing route adjustments into their terminals.

Marcus arrived at six in the morning on his first Monday, wearing the pressed white dress shirt and clean slacks from his cardboard box. Clipping his newly issued employee badge to his breast pocket, he sat down at a workstation near the east window. The desk held two monitors, a dispatch radio handset, and a laminated route map of the municipal system.

His supervisor, a heavyset man wearing a transit authority polo, pointed at the screen.

"You operate the 720 and the 754 lines for the morning shift. The system flags any vehicle running more than eight minutes behind schedule in red. You call the driver, identify the delay, and execute a reroute."

Marcus nodded. His fingers moved across the keyboard with the precise, automatic rhythm of a man who had spent twelve years managing freight logistics. Dispatching municipal bus routes operated on the exact mechanical principles as dispatching commercial cargo: track the asset, identify the delay, calculate the fastest alternative path, execute the correction. His hands knew the work before his conscious mind engaged with the screen.

By Wednesday, his supervisor stopped checking his terminal. Marcus cleared a four-vehicle pileup on Wilshire Boulevard by rerouting three bus lines through side streets within six minutes of the incident report. He handled a broken rail switch near the Seventh Street station by coordinating a bus bridge that moved nine hundred passengers in forty-five minutes. The dispatch floor operated at a pace that left no room for anxiety. When his hands and his attention locked onto the logistics grid, the memory of the supermarket parking lot receded.

His first paycheck arrived on the fifteenth of the month. He stared at the direct-deposit confirmation on his phone screen. It covered rent, groceries, fuel, and vehicle insurance, providing a thin but secure margin.

The following Saturday, Marcus met with an MDI housing coordinator at the ground-floor intake desk. Avoiding the speculative real estate market that caused his initial displacement, he signed a lease for a municipal workforce housing unit located seven miles east of the tower. The civic infrastructure owned the building, locking his rent permanently at thirty percent of his transit authority income. This structural mechanism severed his vulnerability to private rent spikes and profit-driven evictions.

He rode the elevator to the fourth floor, walked into his room, and packed his cardboard box. Stripping the sheets from the bed, he carried them to the communal laundry room and ran a wash cycle. When the sheets dried, he folded them and left them stacked on the bare mattress.

Marcus handed his digital keycard to the intake coordinator. His name disappeared from the fourth-floor residential roster. The room he vacated would be cleaned, restocked, and reassigned to the next economic casualty within twenty-four hours. The rapid-exit node operated on strict turnover discipline. A functional room sitting empty represented a displaced citizen sleeping in a vehicle.

Walking through the lobby toward the heavy glass doors, Marcus experienced no physiological alarm. The ground floor layout utilized wide sightlines, eliminating blind corners where a predatory ambush could occur. His nervous system no longer required the architectural reassurance. He pushed through the doors and stepped into the afternoon sun.

His sedan sat in the visitor lot. He drove east on the freeway, exited at Fremont Avenue, and pulled into the designated parking space in front of his municipal unit. Turning off the engine, he sat for a moment in the quiet. He walked to the front door and turned the brass key in the deadbolt.

The lock engaged with a heavy click. Marcus stepped inside, set the cardboard box on the kitchen counter, and closed the door behind him. The rapid exit protocol executed its function.
