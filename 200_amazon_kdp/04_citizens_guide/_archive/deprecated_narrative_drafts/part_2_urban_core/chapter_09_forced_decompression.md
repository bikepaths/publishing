# Chapter 9: Metabolic Stabilization

The stabilization suite measured twelve feet by fourteen feet, designed with the quiet comfort of a premium hotel. Behind smooth acoustic wall panels, an advanced environmental control system maintained the air at a steady sixty-eight degrees in silence. A private bathroom, equipped with a full bath and regulated hot water, sat behind a sliding door. Natural sunlight entered through a sealed, double-glazed window. A living wall of low-maintenance ferns occupied the panel beside the door, executing the biophilic design specification required on every residential floor. Operating as a medical sanctuary engineered to the exact biological specifications that street survival destroys, the room contained no carceral elements.

Jenifer arrived under the legal authority of the CARE Court petition. The intake team had powered down the wall-mounted media screen and the network access points. They provided her damaged sensory circuits with total rest.

She screamed for the first six hours.

Pounding her fists against the heavy wood-veneer door until her knuckles split, she eventually collapsed onto the padded edge of the bed. The acoustic insulation absorbed her voice like a sponge. In the corridor outside, a nurse standing three feet from the door heard nothing louder than a faint, rhythmic thump.

The walls carried a sound transmission class rating of sixty-five. In construction terms, STC-65 insulation blocks sound transmission with the density required to muffle heavy industrial machinery. The rating exists in the specification because the decompression wing houses multiple individuals in simultaneous crisis. Acoustic leakage between suites would transform the floor into a feedback loop of escalating terror. Each suite functions as an independent, sealed acoustic chamber. Jenifer's screaming could not reach the woman recovering in the adjacent unit. That woman's weeping could not reach Jenifer.

A biometric sensor strip embedded in the bed frame tracked Jenifer's heart rate, respiratory rhythm, and gross body movement. The sensor required no physical contact. The data streamed in real time to a monitoring station at the nurses' desk forty feet down the corridor. Medical staff watched her vital signs on a backlit screen, updating her chart every fifteen minutes. They did not enter the room.

For an individual whose amygdala has been battered by years of street exposure, any human voice, clipboard, or direct eye contact is processed as an immediate, lethal threat. The brain cannot heal if the autonomic nervous system remains locked in a state of alarm. By sealing the room against the low-frequency rumble of the city and the high-frequency footsteps in the corridor, the architecture removed all threat vectors from the environment. The biometric sensor monitored the resident without requiring a nurse to open the door. This eliminated the startle responses that reset the biological clock of panic. The silence acted as a clinical shield, allowing the adrenal system to stand down.

By the ninth hour, Jenifer stopped pounding. She sat on the edge of the mattress, her bleeding hands pressed flat against her thighs. Her chest heaved in shallow, rapid bursts. Her eyes swept the room in frantic arcs, scanning the corners, the ceiling vent, and the narrow window. She searched for the threat her amygdala insisted was present.

The threat did not materialize. No guard opened the door. No siren pierced the walls. No stranger's cough echoed through a shared dormitory. The room offered nothing for her survival circuitry to lock onto.

At hour fourteen, her breathing pattern shifted. The biometric strip recorded a gradual deceleration from thirty-two breaths per minute to twenty-four. Her heart rate dropped from one hundred and eighteen beats per minute to ninety-six. These numbers remained elevated by clinical standards, but they represented the first measurable reduction in sympathetic nervous system output Jenifer's body had produced in over five years of continuous exposure.

She lay down on her side, pulling her knees toward her chest. Her swollen hands curled loosely against the white cotton sheet. She did not sleep. Her eyelids remained half-open, tracking the thin bar of light as it crept slowly across the paneled wall with the rotation of the earth. She failed to sustain the metabolic cost of full-spectrum panic.

A meal tray arrived through a dual-door pass-through slot built into the wall. The outer door locked automatically when the inner door opened, preserving the acoustic seal. Warm rice, steamed vegetables, and a sealed container of electrolyte solution sat on a molded plastic surface. Jenifer did not touch the food. She stared at the tray from the mattress for three hours before her hand moved toward the warm container. Gripping the electrolyte bottle, she brought it to her cracked lips and drank in long, desperate pulls.

On the second morning, the screaming did not resume. Jenifer sat upright on the mattress, her blanket pulled tightly around her shoulders, rocking in a slow, rhythmic pattern. The biometric readout showed her respiratory rate holding steady at twenty breaths per minute. Her heart rate hovered at eighty-eight. The rocking functioned as a primitive self-soothing mechanism her nervous system had retrieved from developmental memory, activated now that the constant external threat bombardment had been architecturally removed.

The quiet walls held. The silence pressed inward. Seventy-two hours after the compelled clinical pathway began, Jenifer's body began the slow, involuntary process of metabolic surrender to safety.
