# Chapter 13: The Dunbar Metric

A municipal shelter containing five hundred beds mathematically guarantees violence. A stabilization pod containing fifteen beds mathematically positions a population for healing. The difference between these two outcomes relies on neurobiology. When a city ignores the mechanical limits of the human brain, the city engineers violence. 

Anthropological research established a fundamental cognitive metric. The human neocortex possesses a strict physical limit regarding social tracking. The brain can maintain stable trust relationships with a maximum of one hundred and fifty individuals. Evolutionary biology hardwired this limitation into the human nervous system. When a facility forces three hundred traumatized individuals into a single warehouse environment, the population massively exceeds this biological limit. 

Mass congregate living guarantees clinical failure. When a brain cannot track who belongs to a group and who presents an external threat, the default biological response is hostility. A person surrounded by three hundred strangers exists in a state of permanent defensive hypervigilance. The amygdala perceives every unmapped individual as a potential predator. This chronic threat perception generates the pervasive violence, theft, and institutional non-compliance observed in mega-shelters. The residents are not failing the shelter. The shelter architecture is failing their neurobiology. 

Material Dignity Infrastructure solves this failure by establishing the Dunbar Pod as the primary architectural unit. A Dunbar Pod houses twelve to twenty individuals. This specific scale mirrors the most intimate evolutionary human group structure. The residents share a central thermal and nutritional core. This shared center radiates outward to private, acoustically isolated sleeping modules and secure sanitation facilities. The architecture fosters communal engagement during meals while guaranteeing absolute privacy during sleep. 

Grouping individuals into these pods requires precise clinical sorting. MDI uses the Structural Assignment Algorithm to populate each Dunbar Pod. A system cannot place residents together randomly. The algorithmic index evaluates specific volatility markers and cohesion tracking data. It separates highly volatile individuals to prevent physical confrontation. It groups individuals with complementary stability metrics to encourage peer regulation. The system engineers social trust. 

Social cohesion does not emerge as an accidental byproduct of compatible personalities. It functions as a strict engineering output. When an infrastructure project matches human density to biological tracking capacity, the environment generates trust. When the infrastructure exceeds that capacity, the environment generates violence. Healing requires a physical space that a damaged brain can accurately map. The Dunbar Pod provides that map.
