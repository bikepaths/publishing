# Philosophical Anchors: The Moral Physics of Survival

## 1. Classical Stoicism and Secular Natural Law

Classical Natural Law theory posits that moral principles are discoverable through human reason because the universe possesses an inherent rational structure. The Stoics referred to this rational ordering principle as the *Logos*; Cicero translated this concept into Roman jurisprudence as *lex naturalis*, defining it as right reason in agreement with nature. 

Key historical reference points:
*   **Cicero (*De Re Publica*)**: Cicero argued that true law is universal, eternal, and unchanging; it binds all nations at all times. Civil authority cannot abrogate natural law; individual conscience and reason serve as the direct link to discovering these universal obligations.
*   **Stoic Cosmopolitanism**: The Stoics asserted that because all human beings share in the rational capacity of the *Logos*, every individual possesses inherent moral worth; this creates a universal community (*cosmopolis*) that transcends local political and tribal boundaries.
*   **Secularization Thesis**: Hugo Grotius later established that natural law would retain its validity even if God did not exist (*etiamsi daremus non esse Deum*); this framework grounds moral duties purely in human nature and the social impulse.

## 2. David Hume and the Is-Ought Gap

In *A Treatise of Human Nature*, David Hume identified the logical fallacy of transitioning from descriptive statements about what is to normative statements about what ought to be. This logical barrier remains the primary challenge to naturalistic ethics.

Conceptual resolutions for the manuscript:
*   **Constitutive Rules and Institutional Facts**: John Searle demonstrated that certain factual descriptions imply normative commitments. Within structured institutions, entering a contract or making a promise constitutes a factual description that carries an immediate deontic obligation.
*   **Biological Needs and Functioning**: Philippa Foot argued that moral evaluations resemble biological evaluations. Just as a physical organism requires specific functions to be considered healthy, a rational human agent requires cooperative behaviors and protections to operate; the descriptive fact of human vulnerability directly determines the prescriptive obligations of care.

## 3. The Capabilities Approach: Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum

The capabilities approach evaluates human well-being and justice by measuring what individuals are actually capable of doing and being. This framework shifts focus away from utility or resource distribution.

Normative framework:
*   **Amartya Sen**: Focuses on individual agency and freedom. Sen argues that evaluation must assess the substantive freedoms (capabilities) an individual possesses to choose a life they have reason to value.
*   **Martha Nussbaum**: Proposes a definite list of central human capabilities required for a life of human dignity; these include bodily health, bodily integrity, practical reason, and affiliation. Nussbaum asserts that society is obligated to bring every citizen above a basic threshold of these capabilities.
*   **The Extremis Boundary**: The manuscript applies this approach to cases where the biological floor of agency has been destroyed; in these scenarios, metabolic and cognitive stabilization must precede the development of higher-order capabilities.

## 4. Institutional Capture and the Banality of Evil

Hannah Arendt analyzed how systemic catastrophes are frequently executed by ordinary individuals acting within bureaucratic roles rather than by malicious actors.

Systemic application:
*   **The Eichmann Paradigm**: Arendt identified "thoughtlessness" as the primary source of administrative harm. Eichmann did not act out of ideological hatred; he acted out of an inability to think from the perspective of another person or to challenge institutional rules.
*   **The Revenue Machine**: When applied to the modern state-funded homelessness system, Arendt's framework explains how institutions capture funding, align their internal incentives toward self-preservation, and systematically perpetuate the conditions of displacement while individual employees maintain clear consciences.

## 5. Epidemiology and the Method of John Snow

Dr. John Snow's intervention during the 1854 Broad Street cholera outbreak demonstrates the empirical methodology of moral physics.

Methodological steps:
*   **Eschewing Miasma**: Snow rejected the popular air-quality theory of disease transmission because it lacked predictive power.
*   **Mechanism Identification**: By mapping cholera deaths, Snow identified a spatial correlation with the Broad Street pump.
*   **Direct Intervention**: Snow removed the pump handle; this physical intervention broke the causal chain of transmission.
*   **Moral Physics Application**: The moral physicist does not merely condemn systemic failure; they map the structural flows, locate the causal nodes, and intervene to dismantle the mechanism of harm.
