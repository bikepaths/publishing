The three working papers in this series, Material Dignity Infrastructure: A Distributed Stewardship Model (2025), Structural Misalignment and the Activation of Surplus Shelter Capacity (February 2026), and the Los Angeles Metropolitan Stabilization analysis (May 2026), have been synthesized into a new book-length treatment now available through Kindle Direct Publishing.

**The Weaponization of Compassion: Material Deprivation and the Homeless Industrial Complex** draws on the utility model, street-to-home pipeline specification, and metabolic stabilization framework developed in these working papers and situates them against the peer-reviewed federal evidence record on encampment sweeps, the institutional capture literature, and the D.C. and California municipal data. The book extends the working paper arguments in two directions the papers do not cover: the political economy of the Sacramento funding apparatus, and a sustained engagement with Reinaldo Escobar's formulation that without material dignity, all freedom is a lie, which provides the convergence point for the Arendt and Giddens frameworks already present in the MDI research corpus.

The book is written for a policy professional and academic audience rather than an academic specialist audience. It applies the same evidence base as the working papers but in a register designed for readability without sacrificing analytical precision. Readers who want the full technical specification of the MDI operational model should consult the working papers directly. Readers who want the argument's policy context, historical evidence, and structural critique of the existing intervention apparatus should begin with the book.

## Podcast and Outreach Hook

*For use in podcast pitches, interview requests, and policy conference abstracts.*

California spent thirty-seven billion dollars on homelessness between 2019 and 2024. The unsheltered population grew throughout the entire period. Charles J. DiBella, Principal Systems Architect of the Material Dignity Infrastructure project and the author of Building Material Dignity, argues in his new book that this outcome is structural rather than accidental. The Homeless Industrial Complex, he contends, is not failing its stated mission. It is succeeding at a different one. Drawing on thirty years of participant observation across the unsheltered ecologies of three continents, peer-reviewed medical evidence from JAMA and BMC Public Health, federal HUD and USICH guidance, and the political philosophy of Hannah Arendt and the lived testimony of Cuban journalist Reinaldo Escobar, DiBella proposes a specific industrial alternative to the welfare model the evidence has found inadequate: a utility-based street-to-home pipeline with auditable metrics at every stage. The book is the argument the evidence has been building for years.
