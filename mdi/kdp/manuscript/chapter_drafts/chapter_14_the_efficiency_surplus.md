The current system forces taxpayers to bleed money through invisible channels. Los Angeles spends approximately fifty thousand dollars per person per year managing a single individual trapped on the street through fragmented emergency responses: police dispatches, ambulance rides, emergency room visits, court appearances, and sanitation crews repeatedly cleaning the same encampments. This massive expenditure purchases zero stabilization. The person cycles back to the same corner the following morning. The city pays full price every single year for a permanent crisis it never solves.

The MDI fiscal architecture attacks this failure through four reinforcing financial layers. The first layer bills Medi-Cal for Phase Zero metabolic stabilization at the sub-acute specialty mental health rate, generating approximately fifteen thousand dollars per enrolled resident during the sixty-day stabilization episode, with roughly half of that funded directly by the federal government. The second layer bills Medi-Cal for ongoing clinical services once a resident moves into the tower at one hundred and fifty to one hundred and ninety dollars per enrolled client per day. These two layers fund the most medically intensive phases of the pipeline under existing California law, requiring no new legislation.

The third layer draws from Measure Alpha and the Mental Health Services Act to fund the field architecture, the Pod Stewards, and the residential management of the building. The fourth layer generates the efficiency surplus by eliminating the fifty-thousand-dollar annual street management cost per resident. When a person moves into the tower, the police stop responding to their bridge and the ambulance stops transporting them to the emergency room. That recovered cash flows directly into the building's operating account.

The math produces a specific recovery timeline verified against the spec. The prototype at One California Plaza costs one hundred and ninety-five million dollars in total capital expenditure. Two thousand housed residents each eliminate fifty thousand dollars in annual street costs, generating one hundred million dollars of recovered expenditure in the first year of full operation. The city recovers the full capital investment within twenty-nine months of tower activation. The building pays for itself before the first residents have lived there for two and a half years.

The financial architecture resolves two crises simultaneously. It houses two thousand people while providing an orderly resolution to the defaulted three-hundred-million-dollar bank debt on One California Plaza, which the bondholders recover at verified floor value rather than a chaotic bankruptcy loss. The bank avoids a catastrophic write-down. The city acquires its infrastructure at eighty percent below peak value.
