Anonymity acts as a dangerous shield on the street. When a city simply counts the number of tents during an annual census, it fails to understand the actual humans living inside them. A generic spreadsheet cannot track a specific medical history or recognize when a physical condition suddenly worsens. The current system treats the unhoused population like a shifting river of unknown faces. This massive blind spot allows people to disappear and die without anyone noticing. A functioning pipeline requires exact names and precise locations. Anonymity guarantees failure.

The Material Dignity Infrastructure replaces the annual census with a living map. We call this tracking system the By-Name Registry. Instead of counting tents, the system records the exact identity, medical background, and daily location of every individual living outside. The city builds a dedicated file for each person that travels with them across different agencies and hospitals. If a man moves from a bridge to an alley, the system updates his position immediately. The map tracks the human instead of the concrete. Information becomes the first tool of rescue.

A database requires human hands to gather the information. The system deploys specialized units called Assertive Community Treatment teams directly into the camps. These teams do not sit inside air-conditioned offices waiting for desperate people to ask for help. They walk into the underground city every single day to build trust through repeated, predictable contact. The team includes a nurse, a social worker, and an addiction specialist working together on the pavement. They map the survival networks. They learn the names.

Trust operates as the only bridge between the street and the clinic. A person surviving outside for five years views every city worker as a dangerous threat. The outreach teams must prove their reliability by showing up consistently over many months. They provide minor medical care and replace stolen identification cards to demonstrate they can actually solve physical problems. Once the person trusts the team, they finally accept the offer to enter the Phase Zero medical buffer. Trust is a mechanical requirement. You cannot force a frightened person through a door.

The By-Name Registry makes it impossible for the system to ignore the hardest cases. When every person possesses a tracked file, the city can no longer hide the severely ill individuals who repeatedly refuse to enter a shelter. The database flashes a warning light when a person remains trapped outside for too long. This exact tracking forces the city to shift from voluntary charity to compelled medical intervention for the individuals actively dying on the street. The map creates total accountability.
