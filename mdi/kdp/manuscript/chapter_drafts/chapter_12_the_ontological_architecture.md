A physical building only works if people agree to walk through the front door, and the existing shelter system consistently generates rational refusal by demanding that individuals surrender their three primary survival anchors as the price of admission. Understanding why these anchors exist requires understanding what the street does to attachment behavior. A person who has lived outside for several years develops survival bonds of unusual intensity: to their animal companion, who provides emotional regulation and physical warmth without the threat and unpredictability of other humans; to their intimate partner, because facing the street alone amplifies the risk of violence, theft, and exposure mortality; and to their accumulated possessions, which represent the entirety of their remaining material capital. These are not preferences. They are survival architecture. Demanding that a person abandon them to access a shelter bed is functionally equivalent to demanding that they abandon their survival strategy to access a service claiming to help them.

The Three Ps infrastructure solves each barrier through direct physical design rather than policy exceptions or case-by-case waivers. The pet barrier resolves through a professionally managed kennel facility on the second floor of the tower. Each animal companion occupies an individual climate-controlled enclosed run. The facility is sound-dampened and odor-managed to maintain residential quality on adjacent floors. Resident access runs from six in the morning until ten at night at zero cost to the resident. An on-site non-profit veterinary partnership provides all medical care, vaccinations, behavioral assessment, and spay-neuter services. The animal never leaves the building. The resident does not separate from the animal. The barrier dissolves because the obstacle itself no longer exists in the architecture.

Partners receive two adjacent ALMUs within the same Dunbar Pod, matched at intake through the HMIS system. Two locked doors positioned side by side allow each individual to maintain their own sovereign private space while remaining within meters of their partner. The couple does not surrender their relationship. They gain individual privacy that chronic co-habitation in a tent or under a bridge cannot provide. The possessions barrier resolves through a ground-floor secure storage system with individual bays that are photographed, tagged with GPS trackers, and accessible by personal RFID card around the clock. No staff member can access a resident's storage bay without the resident's explicit consent. The stored possessions do not diminish or disappear. They wait.

The ALMU itself, the one hundred and fifty net square feet of private space behind the hard, solid-core door with an STC 65 acoustic frame and threshold seal, is the clinical instrument that does the heaviest therapeutic work in the entire building. The Murphy wall-bed folds flat when stowed, returning approximately thirty-five square feet of floor space during waking hours. A wall-mounted fold-down desk with seated and standing configurations sits adjacent to under-desk locking storage. A one-point-seven cubic foot mini-refrigerator, an induction cooktop plate, and a microwave alcove allow the resident to prepare food independently. Gas is prohibited at this density as an absolute fire safety constraint. A built-in wardrobe column twelve inches deep and twenty-four inches wide accommodates the resident's clothing. The wet bath occupying twenty-four to twenty-eight square feet functions as a single waterproofed chamber where the toilet, wall-mounted sink, and showerhead share a drain-sloped floor. Resident-controlled water temperature draws from a solar thermal pre-heated supply. This private bathroom is a non-negotiable dignity threshold. No communal hygiene facilities exist in the building at any level.

Every ALMU that occupies a perimeter position receives full-height commercial glazing with floor-to-ceiling sky views. Where interior units are necessary to reach the two-thousand-unit target, full-spectrum circadian LED ceiling panels execute a programmable cycle from dawn through midday to dusk over a twenty-four-hour period, providing documented mitigation of seasonal affective disorder and the circadian disruption produced by years of unstructured outdoor sleep patterns. Unobstructed sky access, whether through a window or through a calibrated artificial equivalent, is a therapeutic specification, because individuals exiting street encampments where their entire spatial experience has been outdoor and open-sky require a visual connection to natural light cycles as part of their neurological reorientation. The architecture of the ALMU does not merely shelter the person. It systematically dismantles, one physical specification at a time, the biological mechanisms that made street survival feel safer than indoor stability.
