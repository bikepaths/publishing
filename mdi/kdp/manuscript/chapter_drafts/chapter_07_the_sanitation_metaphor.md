London faced a biological emergency during the summer of 1854. A cholera outbreak in the Soho district killed more than six hundred people in ten days, and nobody in city government understood why. The prevailing scientific theory of the time blamed "miasma," the idea that disease spread through foul-smelling air produced by rotting organic material. City officials responded by distributing moral pamphlets encouraging cleanliness and personal responsibility. They preached hygiene to a population drinking water that carried the bacterium killing them. The death toll climbed because the solution targeted the wrong variable.

Physician John Snow conducted the investigation that broke the miasma theory. He mapped every cholera death in Soho on a street grid and discovered that cases clustered around a single water pump on Broad Street. He persuaded the local council to remove the pump handle. The outbreak collapsed within days. Snow had not cured cholera through moral argument or behavioral change. He had identified the physical transmission mechanism and severed it through a mechanical intervention. The science of sanitation, and eventually the full underground sewer grid that engineer Joseph Bazalgette completed beneath London over the following decade, eliminated cholera from the city not by changing how Londoners behaved but by redesigning the infrastructure their bodies depended on.

The parallel to the modern street crisis is structural rather than superficial. City governments respond to visible chronic homelessness primarily through two instruments: outreach workers distributing temporary supplies, and law enforcement operations clearing encampments from visible public spaces. Both instruments target the symptoms of the crisis while leaving its physical causes intact. An outreach worker distributing socks and food on a Tuesday morning is the nineteenth-century equivalent of distributing moral pamphlets. The gesture is humane. It does not modify the underlying infrastructure. The person receiving the socks sleeps on the pavement again Tuesday night.

The sanitation framework requires identifying the transmission mechanism rather than managing the symptoms it produces. The cholera epidemic was not caused by the behavior of Soho residents. It was caused by the physical co-location of sewage and drinking water in the same infrastructure. Chronic street homelessness in Los Angeles is not caused by the behavior of unhoused individuals, though their behavior is dramatically shaped by their physical conditions. It is caused by the physical absence of the metabolic stabilization layer that would convert acute crisis into recoverable stability. The system delivers housing to people in acute physiological failure and then measures the predictable retention failure as evidence of population intractability.

Bazalgette's sewer grid cost the equivalent of approximately three billion dollars in modern currency and took eleven years to complete between 1859 and 1870. It processed the sewage of a city of three million people and eliminated the recurring cholera epidemics that had killed tens of thousands. The upfront capital cost was enormous by the standards of its era. The alternative, continuing to manage cholera outbreaks through charity and enforcement, cost more in medical expenditure, lost labor productivity, and social disruption over a shorter time window than the infrastructure build. The engineering solution was cheaper than the crisis management solution. The same arithmetic applies to the MDI tower.

The Material Dignity Infrastructure does not propose charity at industrial scale. It proposes infrastructure at industrial scale, specifically the construction of the physical pipeline that currently does not exist between acute street crisis and stable residential tenure. Snow's pump handle removal was not a permanent solution. It stopped one outbreak. The sewer grid was the permanent solution because it addressed the causal mechanism rather than the symptom.
