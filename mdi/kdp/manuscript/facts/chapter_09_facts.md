# Chapter 9 Atomic Facts

## Section 1: The Point-in-Time Count Limitations
- The Los Angeles Homeless Services Authority's point-in-time count deploys volunteers on a single night in January to estimate the number of people sleeping outside.
- The point-in-time count does not record names, medical histories, behavioral patterns, survival anchors, or preferred locations.
- The aggregate count does not track reasons for individual long-term homelessness.

## Section 2: The By-Name HMIS Registry
- The By-Name HMIS Registry replaces the aggregate count with a database of individual files.
- Field documentation teams build files for unhoused individuals within the Skid Row target zone during a six-month pre-operation period.
- Each file records a name or descriptor, a GPS location pattern, survival anchors (such as animal companions and possessions), a pipeline classification, and contact notes.
- The database is managed by the MDI Stewardship Authority and shared across partner agencies in real time.
- The database updates when an individual moves locations.

## Section 3: The Houston HMIS Model
- Houston's HMIS network spans more than one hundred partner agencies.
- Houston achieved a sixty percent encampment reduction using its unified HMIS database.
- A unified database preserves client relationship histories across different agencies and staff transitions.

## Section 4: ACT Team Caseloads and Composition
- The Assertive Community Treatment (ACT) team manages caseloads of eighty to one hundred twenty Pipeline C individuals.
- ACT team composition includes: one mobile psychiatrist, one registered nurse, one licensed social worker, one outreach case manager, one peer support specialist (with lived experience of homelessness or psychiatric illness), one substance use counselor, and one team coordinator.
- Redundant staffing coverage prevents relationship disruption due to staff turnover.
- Peer specialists establish initial contact for individuals with paranoid presentations who reject institutional authority figures.
- Psychiatrists and social workers follow the relationship established by the peer specialist.

## Section 5: The Operational Engagement Protocol
- Initial contacts provide coffee, food, wound care, and harm reduction materials without intake forms, entry conditions, or housing offers.
- Teams maintain a predictable schedule of visits to encampments.
- Predictable schedules lower the threat-perception baseline of paranoid individuals.
- Medical interventions follow initial engagement, starting with wound treatment and progressing to medication assistance.
- Housing offers are presented after teams identify behavioral signals of willingness, which often follow traumatic encampment events (such as nearby deaths or violence).

## Section 6: Pre-Confirmed Placements and Elimination of Friction
- The housing offer includes a pre-confirmed unit number in the HMIS system.
- Kennel spaces for animal companions are reserved in advance of the housing offer.
- Possessions are photographed, tagged, GPS-tracked, and stored in spaces accessible by RFID card.
- Intake documentation, benefits enrollment, and housing agreements are completed before the housing offer is presented to eliminate bureaucratic delay.
