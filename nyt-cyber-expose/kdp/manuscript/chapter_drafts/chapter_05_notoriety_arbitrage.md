# Chapter Five: Notoriety Arbitrage

The Akhter brothers did not attempt to vanish after deleting ninety‑six federal databases. Instead they left traces—some deliberate, some inadvertent—that would eventually lead investigators to their doorsteps. This was not a failure of tradecraft. It was a calculated decision rooted in a concept that appears in both criminal justice and insurgency literature: notoriety arbitrage. Notoriety arbitrage occurs when the perceived value of being known to have executed an operation—whether for financial gain, reputation leverage, or psychological satisfaction—exceeds the expected cost of attribution and prosecution. In the Akhter case the brothers weighed the potential benefits of claiming responsibility (or leaving evidence that pointed to them) against the risk of spending decades in federal prison. In real time during the operation they made a series of micro‑decisions that reveal how that calculus unfolded. To understand notoriety arbitrage one must first step outside the traditional deterrence model that dominates cybersecurity policy. Standard deterrence assumes that actors are rational, seek to avoid detection, and weigh the probability of capture against the potential gain from a successful operation. If the expected cost (probability of punishment × severity of penalty) exceeds the expected benefit, the actor is deterred. This model works well for many financially motivated cybercrimes where anonymity is paramount—credit‑card fraud, identity theft, ransomware payments routed through cryptocurrency mixers. But it begins to fray when the actor’s goals extend beyond pure financial gain to include elements of recognition, retaliation, or strategic signaling—when the act itself becomes a message and the value of that message is not diminished but potentially enhanced by attribution. The Akhters’ case provides a rare window into this phenomenon because the brothers documented their own deliberations in real time. At approximately 5:12 p.m. on February 18 2025—while the deletion sequence was still underway and dozens of databases had already been dropped—Sohaib Akhter turned to his brother and said, according to the Statement of Facts: “you shoulda had a kill script like blackmailing them for some money…”. Muneeb declined the suggestion not on moral grounds but on evidentiary ones: engaging in extortion or leaving a ransom note would create an obvious prosecutable link between the breach and their identities. In that moment the brothers performed a live risk assessment: the potential financial gain from blackmail (perhaps tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars) was outweighed by the near‑certainty that such a move would trigger an immediate, intense investigation that would likely result in life sentences. They chose instead to let the operation speak for itself—through the sheer scale of the deletion and the subsequent credential‑stuffing campaign—while avoiding any direct communication that could be used as evidence of intent to profit. This exchange is significant not because it reveals a moral boundary but because it shows that the brothers were actively evaluating trade‑offs during the operation itself. They were not blindly following a script. They were weighing the marginal utility of each additional action—delete another database, exfiltrate another file, consider a blackmail demand—against the increasing risk of attribution. The fact that they discussed the option at all proves that notoriety was on the table as a potential asset. They ultimately decided that the value of remaining anonymous (or more accurately of not providing prosecutors with a smoking gun) exceeded the short‑term financial upside of claiming responsibility via extortion. Yet notoriety arbitrage is not a binary choice between total secrecy and full confession. It exists on a spectrum, and the Akhter case illustrates how actors can pursue partial attribution—leaving enough clues to satisfy a psychological need for recognition or to send a message to a specific audience—while still avoiding the evidentiary threshold required for conviction. In the days and weeks following the breach the brothers engaged in behavior that suggests they wanted the operation to be discovered but not necessarily traced back to them personally. The exfiltration of 1,805 EEOC complaint files and 450 taxpayer records, for example, was not necessary for the destruction mission. It could have been omitted if the sole goal was to cause disruption. Yet they took the time to copy those files to a USB drive—a decision that implies a desire for the data to exist somewhere beyond the compromised systems—perhaps as leverage, perhaps as a trophy, perhaps as a way to signal capability to a third party. Similarly, Muneeb’s retention of his PIV card—a high‑security smart card that grants physical access to federal facilities—and his subsequent flight to Texas with it while not directly profitable served as a tangible proof of access that could be used in negotiations or as a marker of accomplishment. The brothers’ prior conduct offers further insight into their mindset. In their 2014‑2015 State Department hack they did not merely exfiltrate data. They also engaged in behavior that suggested a desire for recognition within certain circles. According to court documents from that case they shared screenshots of their access with acquaintances and discussed the possibility of selling the data to foreign actors—though no evidence of an actual transaction was found. This pattern of operating in the shadows while flirting with the edges of attribution is consistent with a mindset that values both operational success and the reputational capital that comes from being known—within certain bounds—to have pulled it off. The deception gap in notoriety arbitrage often lies in the difference between what an actor wants to communicate and what they are willing to prove in a court of law. The Akhters may have wanted the world to know that a contractor employee could walk in and delete nearly a hundred federal databases in under an hour—but they were not willing to leave a signed confession or engage in conduct that would make prosecution trivial. Their actions therefore were optimized for a different objective: to create an event so large, so disruptive, and so undeniably linked to their access that it would force a reckoning—while still preserving enough ambiguity to avoid immediate open‑and‑shut culpability. This is a sophisticated form of signaling: not a declaration of responsibility but a demonstration of capability that invites the observer to connect the dots without giving the prosecutor a document that says “I did it.” When the brothers’ guilty pleas and sentencing recommendations are considered, the notoriety arbitrage framework helps explain why the operation proceeded despite the severe penalties on the table. Muneeb faces up to 45 years in prison if sentenced consecutively on all counts. Sohaib, convicted on May 7 2026, faces up to 21 years. These are not trivial exposures. Yet the brothers proceeded anyway—suggesting that in their calculation the expected value of the operation (in terms of disruption capability, demonstration, psychological satisfaction, or potential leverage from the exfiltrated data) exceeded the expected cost even when adjusted for the high probability of eventual attribution and conviction. The fact that they discussed blackmail and dismissed it on evidentiary—not moral—grounds indicates that they were not deterred by the prospect of prison. They were deterred by the prospect of making the prosecution’s job too easy. From a deterrence‑theory perspective this reveals a limitation in how we model insider threat. Traditional models assume that actors seek to minimize the probability of detection. But the Akhter case suggests that some actors may actually seek to optimize for detectability up to a point—wanting the breach to be large enough to be noticed, to trigger an investigation, to create a systemic wake‑up call—while still avoiding the specific actions that would make attribution trivial. This is not pure rational deterrence. It is a more nuanced calculation where the actor values the systemic impact of the breach as much as, or more than, the secrecy of their involvement. In other words they wanted the architecture to be exposed—not necessarily themselves. To close this gap would require rethinking how we approach insider‑threat prevention in environments where actors may not be seeking anonymity. One strategy would be to shift focus from preventing detection to preventing the operational success itself—by implementing controls that make large‑scale deletion or exfiltration technically difficult regardless of intent. Examples include: mandatory multi‑person approval for `DROP DATABASE` commands on production systems; behavioral analytics that flag unusual query patterns (such as a contractor engineer issuing dozens of deletion statements in a short window); air‑gapped backups that cannot be deleted from the primary environment; and automated privileged‑access termination that severs credentials the moment an employment status change is detected in HR systems. Another strategy would be to increase the likelihood of attribution through improved logging—such as immutable audit logs, geofencing of database access, and mandatory MFA for privileged actions—so that even if an actor wants the breach to be noticed, the cost of acting rises because the investigator can quickly trace the activity back to a specific individual. Neither approach is free. Each would require investment, process change, and potential friction with legitimate operations. But as the Akhter case demonstrates, the cost of assuming all insider threats seek anonymity is already being measured in deleted databases, exfiltrated personal records, and the lingering question of how many other actors might be willing to trade secrecy for spectacle—or for a message that only they understand.
