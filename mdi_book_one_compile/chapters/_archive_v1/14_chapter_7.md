# Chapter 7: The Infinite Spending Drain

Big cities fund a system built to move the problem rather than fix it. This old setup does not help the people since it only pushes the crisis to a new block. Cities spend hundreds of millions of dollars every year on street sweeps to clear tents away from stores. The old system calls this public safety whereas it is really just about how the city looks. We call this Aesthetic Management because the goal is a clean view while the human crisis remains untouched.

A single move of a large camp costs millions of dollars in extra city fees. Police must show up to give notice and guard the area. Cleaning crews arrive with big machines to take away everything the people own. Trucks take the trash and the property to storage centers. Street cleaners wash the sidewalk so it looks nice for the nearby stores. The city spends this money even though they know it will not solve anything. The move creates a clean sidewalk for a short time whereas the people just move a few blocks away. Within days, the tents are back and the cycle starts again.

This cycle is an Infinite Spending Drain. The city spends its money on a process that is built to repeat itself forever. The people on the street have no other place to go because the city is not fixing the real problems. They have no homes and no families to help them. A police sweep just pushes them to a new sidewalk. The sidewalk clears for a moment whereas the crisis continues. The budget resets and the city pays for the same move all over again.

The public pays for this cycle forever. The old system keeps this machine running because it creates quick results that look good on camera. The rules ignore the fact that the people have moved around the corner. The spending continues because it delivers a short-term clean street rather than a permanent physical fix. The city buys a temporary view with money that should be used for real buildings. This way of operating leads to a zero percent return on the public's money.
