# Chapter 3: The Mathematical Output

Modern street tents first appeared in large numbers during the early 1980s. This sudden growth was the exact result of three major problems hitting the city at once. First, local laws destroyed cheap single rooms which were the last safe places for poor people to live. Second, the federal budget made deep cuts to local safety nets. Third, family networks were worn out and could no longer help their relatives. The small family unit broke apart under this massive pressure because it had no other way to survive.

When the large family network collapsed, the city faced a crisis it did not understand. The old system failed to see that the physical safety net was gone. The machine labeled the people on the street as a collection of sick and lazy individuals. The old rules treated the crisis as a problem of drug use and mental failure whereas it was physically a failure of homes. This bad map allowed the system to blame the person rather than the broken blueprint. It forced the heavy weight of survival onto human bodies that were already exhausted by the struggle to live.

The physical reality of being poor does not care about political stories. A person who has no home and no money cannot stay stable through willpower alone. When the family safety net breaks, the citizen falls onto the sidewalk. The street tent acts as the final collection point for people who have nothing else left to hold them up. Asking these people to fix their lives through good behavior goes against the laws of nature. The city cannot use arrests or doctors to solve a problem caused by missing infrastructure. Voters must see that the person on the street is the inevitable result of a society that has removed all its safety nets.
