# Preface

I have been watching people die on American streets for thirty years. The watching was not passive. I worked in the systems that were supposed to prevent the dying, and I watched those systems process human beings through protocols that accelerated the damage they were designed to repair. The experience left me with a distinct conviction: the crisis on our sidewalks is not a mystery. It is a design failure, and design failures have design solutions.

This book is the design solution. It translates three decades of firsthand observation and five years of structured research into a blueprint that any city can build. The research spans neurobiology, stress physiology, urban economics, commercial real estate analysis, and the political philosophy that governs what a society owes to the people it has failed to protect. The bibliography at the end of this volume lists the sources. The chapters that follow distill them into an argument that does not require a graduate degree to follow.

I wrote the first version of this material as a series of working papers and academic manuscripts. The working papers accomplished what working papers are designed to accomplish: they established the evidentiary foundation and subjected the claims to formal scrutiny. What they did not accomplish was communication. A working paper written in academic register reaches the twelve people who review it and the two hundred who cite it. The person who votes for the city council member who funds the budget that operates the system that produces the outcomes documented in the working paper never reads it. The gap between the research and the voter is the gap this book exists to close.

The argument in these pages is not compassionate. Compassion is a feeling, and feelings do not build buildings. The argument is mechanical. A human body requires distinct physical conditions to remain functional: temperature regulation, caloric intake, clean water, sanitation, sleep in an acoustically isolated space behind a locked door, and a sustained period of safety long enough for the stress hormones that have saturated the body's tissue to normalize. These are engineering specifications. When they are met, the body recovers. When they are not met, the body deteriorates on a timeline that stress physiologists have documented in clinical detail. The city's choice is not whether to feel sympathy for the person on the sidewalk. The city's choice is whether to spend less money on a system that works or more money on a system that does not.

I did not write this book for people who already agree with me. I wrote it for the taxpayer who drives past the encampment on the way to work and wants to know why twenty-four billion dollars in state spending has not produced a visible reduction in the number of tents. The answer is in these pages. The spending did not work because it was spent on the wrong things, built on the wrong assumptions, and measured by the wrong metrics. The right things, the right assumptions, and the right metrics are specified in the chapters that follow.

Every dollar figure in this book comes from public records, audited financial reports, or peer-reviewed economic analysis. Every clinical claim is sourced to published research in neurobiology, stress physiology, or gerontology. Every architectural specification is derived from engineering standards that exist in building codes and acoustic performance ratings. I have no interest in persuading anyone through rhetoric. The facts are sufficient. If the facts are not sufficient, no rhetoric will compensate for their absence.

The buildings are standing. The market has priced them within reach. The biology is documented. The legal instruments exist. The budget math works. What remains is the decision to build. This book is the manual for that decision.

Charles J. DiBella
Los Angeles, 2025
