# OVP Audio Track 6: Relational Engineering

**Target Audience**: Practitioners, system architects, and Pod Stewards managing transitional populations.
**Body-First Focus**: The physical stress of isolation vs. the nervous system regulation of trusted, predictable social contact.
**Core Message**: "You cannot scale trust. You can only engineer environments where trust scales itself."
**Systemic Concept Translation**: Relational Dignity Infrastructure (RDI). Moving beyond the Dunbar number requires autonomic routing and formalized, peer-led governance structures.

---

## Episode 1 Dialogue Draft: The Limits of the Pack

**[Ambient Sound: Low murmur of a crowded room. Coffee cups clinking. A steady, calm rhythm.]**

**NARRATOR:**
If you put fifty traumatized strangers in a room, you don't get a community. You get a powder keg. 

The system thinks you can solve isolation by building bigger buildings. They pack three hundred beds into an armory and wonder why the violence spikes. The answer is biological. The human nervous system wasn't built to track three hundred unpredictable threats. 

We are pack animals. We function in small cohorts. When you exceed the cognitive limit of trust—what scientists call the Dunbar number—the brain defaults back to hyper-vigilance. 

**[Beat. The background noise fades into a quiet, focused silence.]**

**NARRATOR:**
If you want to stabilize a population, you have to engineer the relationships, not just the architecture. We break the tower down into pods of twelve. You know everyone in your pod. You eat with them. You govern with them. The Pod Steward isn't a warden; they are a node in a network. 

We call this Relational Engineering. You do not scale a program by making the pods bigger. You scale it by adding more pods. Trust is horizontal, not vertical. If you force a traumatized body into a massive, anonymous crowd, you are actively preventing their nervous system from down-regulating. 

Build the architecture to match the biology, not the budget.
