# 05_INTAKE_TRIAGE: Ground Floor Spatial Architecture, Kinetic Triage, and Metabolic Stabilization
## Material Dignity Infrastructure — From Street Arrival to Residential Threshold
*Feeds: The Tier 1 Ground Floor section of the manuscript.*

---

### Overview: Three Zones, One Floor, Zero Collision

The ground floor of the MDI tower carries three spatially distinct and operationally independent systems on a single 26,000 sq ft floor plate. They co-exist without mixing. The architecture enforces separation; it does not rely on staff enforcement.

**The apparent tension** — an open, welcoming, low-barrier resource center cannot coexist with a secured, clinical intake architecture — resolves at the spatial level. The three zones are:

1. **The Open Resource Center**: The public-facing, welcoming commons. Low-barrier, zero conditions, open to registered guests and upper-floor residents alike.
2. **Zone A — The Sally Port**: Secured, clinical, dedicated arrival node for high-acuity individuals via mobile crisis team or law enforcement handoff. Separate entrance from Zone 1. Not visible from the Resource Center.
3. **Zone B — The Triage/Acuity Segment**: The transitional assessment corridor linking Resource Center to the clinical track. Staffed, informal, conversational — not a clinical gatehouse.

**The design principle**: A voluntarily arriving Pipeline B guest and a mobile-crisis-delivered Pipeline C individual never share a waiting area, a corridor, or a visual field until the latter has been stabilized and transitioned to open flow.

**Upper-floor residents** move freely through the Resource Center — cafeteria, veterinary node, digital hub, decompression zones — as they would any residential ground floor commons. The Resource Center functions as their primary shared amenity space.

---

### The Open Resource Center: Low-Barrier Commons

**Function**: The welcoming face of the building. Open, stress-free, abundant. No triage occurs here. No assessment. No conditions beyond RFID token or thermal scan at the entrance threshold.

**Users**:
- New street arrivals (Pipeline A and B) accessing hygiene, laundry, food, or device charging voluntarily.
- Registered guests exploring the system before any commitment to residency.
- Upper-floor MDI residents using the commons as their residential amenity floor — cafeteria, veterinary node, dog run access, digital access center, decompression zones.
- Visitor meetings: residents may receive guests in the Resource Center commons freely.

**Atmosphere**: Circadian lighting (warm), biophilic materials, curved surfaces, no institutional fixtures. Cafeteria capacity 1,200. Robotic kitchen. Direct-access pharmacy vending. Real-time unit availability display. Veterinary node adjacent to dog run access.

**The Boundary**: The Resource Center has a perimeter. It is not an open street — it is a threshold-controlled commons. RFID token or anonymous thermal scan at entry. No biometric registration. No name required.

---

### The Incident Pathway: Resource Center Back-Door to Zone A

**The Operational Reality**: An incident will occur in the Resource Center. A guest will decompensate, become volatile, or require immediate clinical intervention. The response cannot involve transit through the general commons — that exposure produces cascade escalation.

**The Back-Door Architecture**:
- A dedicated, staff-key-only corridor connects the Resource Center interior directly to Zone A (Sally Port) through a rear-wall passage. Not visible from the general commons floor. No signage.
- When a Pod Steward or Resource Center staff identifies an acute decompensation event, the individual is moved — with calm, conversational guidance and minimal physical contact — through the back-door corridor into Zone A without traversing the Resource Center population.
- The Sally Port interlocking door system then operates normally within Zone A.
- The back-door corridor is staffed by a single peer support specialist at all operating hours — the first human the decompensating individual encounters on transition is someone with lived experience, not a clinician or security officer.

**The Restraint Protocol**: If physical restraint is required before transit through the back-door corridor, the Resource Center has two designated de-escalation alcoves — visually separated from the main commons by biophilic partition walls — where a guest can be attended by two staff members privately until stable enough for back-door transit. Restraint is the last resort; the alcove and peer specialist are the first.

---

### Zone A: The Sally Port (High-Acuity Arrival)

**Function**: Secure, high-flow transfer node for guests arriving in acute psychiatric distress or via mobile crisis team/law enforcement handoff.

**Architecture**:
- Interlocking double-door system (Sally Port) prevents spillover volatility from the arrival zone into the general ground floor.
- Anti-ligature fixtures, tamper-resistant materials, reinforced glazing — integrated into a residential-scale material palette (no correctional aesthetic).
- Dedicated direct access channel for mobile crisis teams and first responders, distinct from the public entrance.
- Clear sightlines from staff node to all positions in Zone A. No blind corners.

**Staffing**: Dual-Role Crisis Node — one CIT-trained (Crisis Intervention Team) behavioral health clinician and one peer support specialist (lived experience). The clinician manages clinical assessment; the peer specialist manages the human connection that reduces threat-perception.

**De-escalation Environment**: Circadian lighting (warm spectrum on arrival, tunable), 20Hz-20kHz acoustic dampening panels, biophilic geometry (curved surfaces, no hard 90-degree corners), and nature-sound atmospheric integration. Empirically documented to reduce cortisol levels in arriving individuals.

---

### Zone B: The Triage/Acuity Segment

**Function**: Immediate "vibe-scan" and informal assessment to route guests into appropriate stabilization pathways.

**Assessment Protocol**: Non-clinical, trauma-informed conversational screening — not a formal intake interview. Staff observe: orientation level, agitation indicators, somatic presentation (wound status, dehydration signs, metabolic distress). Guests are partitioned into:
- **Low-Acuity Flow**: Self-directed access to hygiene, laundry, cafeteria, and decompression zones.
- **High-Acuity Support**: Escorted access to supervised single-occupancy hygiene nodes, wound care, and direct clinical handoff.

**Real-time Unit Availability Display**: Shows unit availability across the tower network. Neutralizes scarcity-driven anxiety before it becomes behavioral agitation.

---

### Acuity-Segmented Hygiene Nodes

**Design Rationale**: Multi-actor escalation in legacy facilities most commonly originates in communal hygiene spaces. A long-term Diogenes-pattern individual in a large open shower facility with other agitated arrivals is a guaranteed flashpoint.

**Specification**:
- Single-occupancy, reinforced medical-grade modules.
- Resident-controlled water temperature defaulting to 38°C (body-adjacent — reduces thermal shock agitation).
- Low-lighting option (overhead fluorescent eliminated; adjustable warm LED).
- No time pressure signaling (no visible countdown timers or staff-initiated exit knocks for first 30 minutes).
- UV-C and HEPA air scrubbing to maintain atmospheric integrity.

**Hygiene Rehabilitation Protocol** (Diogenes-pattern individuals):
- Clothing exchange framed as addition, not replacement. Individual chooses when to transition. Hygiene intake kit (clean clothing, hygiene tools) offered as gift — not as a sanitary requirement.
- Soiled clothing bagged, tagged, and held pending individual consent for disposal. Identity-preserving.
- Skin and wound assessment conducted by clinical staff immediately post-hygiene. Chronic wound pain is a primary driver of behavioral agitation — pain management precedes psychiatric medication adjustment in the stabilization sequence.

### Metabolic Stabilization: The Clinical Sequencing

**Multi-Speed Throughput Regulation**:
To prevent Phase Zero from becoming a throughput bottleneck, the MDI stabilization protocol operates on a **multi-speed track architecture**:
*   **Track 1 (Rapid)**: 7–14 day stabilization for individuals with low psychiatric acuity and primarily metabolic distress.
*   **Track 2 (Standard)**: 30–60 days for typical Pipeline B/C stabilization.
*   **Track 3 (Extended)**: 60–120 days for high-acuity Pipeline C individuals with significant DUP.

By regulating flow based on acuity rather than a fixed timeline, the system prevents backlog formation; furthermore, the ground floor clinical node maintains a **15% overflow capacity** using temporary decompression units to ensure that no high-acuity arrival is turned away during surge events.

**This sequence is non-negotiable. The 72-hour threshold in the MDI spec must reflect the following clinical order:**

**Hour 0-4 — Rehydration (Critical)**:
Oral rehydration must use electrolyte solution (WHO-formulated ORS or equivalent), not plain water. Rapid water loading in severely dehydrated individuals with depleted sodium produces hyponatremia — potentially fatal cerebral edema. This is a life-safety specification for the robotic kitchen and pharmacy protocols.

**Hours 4-24 — Nutritional Triage**:
Refeeding Syndrome is a life-threatening risk for severely malnourished arrivals. Rapid reintroduction of carbohydrates triggers electrolyte shifts producing cardiac arrhythmia and neurological complications. The robotic cafeteria model requires a clinical triage layer that identifies high-risk individuals (BMI <16, visible severe wasting, reported days without food) and routes them to a Gradual Reintroduction Protocol (low-phosphate, low-calorie initial feeding) before open cafeteria access. This is a gap in the current MDI cafeteria specification.

**Hours 24-72 — Psychiatric Assessment Window**:
Once basic metabolic stability is achieved, the individual's neurological baseline rises enough for meaningful psychiatric evaluation. Attempting psychiatric assessment before this window produces false-negative assessments of capacity and compliance. The MDI 72-hour threshold aligns with this clinical logic but must explicitly note: psychiatric assessment follows metabolic stabilization, not concurrent.

### The Rapid Clinical Handoff

For guests requiring immediate psychiatric stabilization beyond ground floor capacity:
- Direct-path access (no general floor traversal) to on-site psychiatric emergency node.
- CIT-trained staff accompany — not security personnel.
- Clinical handoff documented in HMIS with ACT team notification if the individual is a named registry member.
