This is one of the oldest and most difficult questions in political philosophy. It has no clean answer, but it has a precise structure.

**The classical framework**

John Stuart Mill established what is called the *Harm Principle* in 1859. The basic claim is that society is only justified in restricting individual freedom when that individual's actions cause harm to others. Harm to oneself alone is not sufficient justification for intervention.

The problem your question identifies is real: the boundary between self-harm and harm to others is not a line. It is a gradient.

**Where the gradient becomes visible**

Consider the progression:

A person's health deteriorates privately. This affects only themselves. Then untreated wounds produce infection. Infection can transmit. Then waste accumulates. Contaminated water enters shared waterways used by other communities. Then a person in acute psychiatric crisis acts unpredictably in shared public space. Other people's safety is now directly implicated.

At each stage something has crossed from the private body into the shared environment.

**The harder question your framing raises**

The document you uploaded uses exactly this logic. It argues that riparian encampments produce measurable contamination of shared water systems, which triggers federal environmental law independently of any housing or welfare consideration.

This is ethically significant because it bypasses the harder question of personal dignity and reframes the problem as environmental science. The contamination is either present or it is not. The legal threshold is either exceeded or it is not.

But notice what this framing does. It converts a question about human beings into a question about water quality. The person becomes secondary to the measurement.

**The tension this creates**

There are two legitimate claims in direct conflict here. The community downstream has a genuine right to clean water. The person upstream has a genuine claim to dignity and to not be treated as merely a pollution source.

Neither claim cancels the other. A society that ignores the downstream community is failing its members. A society that treats the upstream person purely as a contamination event rather than a human being with a history, an illness, and a set of needs, has also failed morally.

The argument is that you cannot justify clearance without placement, but placement without clearance is also insufficient.
