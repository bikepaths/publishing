**The Em-Dash Problem**

An em-dash in LLM output almost always performs one of three functions. It introduces a parenthetical aside, it pivots to a conclusion, or it creates dramatic pause before an elaboration. In each case the correct fix is to restructure the sentence so the dash is not needed, rather than to substitute a different punctuation mark that performs the same function less elegantly.

For parentheticals — where dashes bracket a phrase in the middle of a sentence — comma pairs almost always work and look entirely natural: "This distinction, between a behavioral refusal and a biological state, is the difference..." A human magazine writer making the same construction would reach for the comma pair without thinking.

For the pivot construction — where a dash precedes a concluding phrase — restructure into a subordinate clause or break at a period: "The offer does not expire. That is the point." rather than "The offer does not expire — that is the point." Where the sentence can absorb the trailing phrase as a subordinate clause with a comma or the word "which," use that.

For the dramatic list — "not X — not Y — not Z" — restore it to a comma-separated list or restructure as parallel clauses. "Her eyes tracked nothing: not the pigeons..." is still a colon. Better: "Her eyes were open but tracked nothing, not the pigeons landing three feet away, not the delivery truck idling at the curb, not the man who said her name."

The rule is that no em-dash gets a replacement. Each one gets a restructured sentence.

**The Broader Fingerprint Set**

Em-dashes are the most visible signal but not the only one. This specific essay has several others worth addressing in sequence.

The "X is not Y. It is Z." construction appears at least four times and is one of the strongest LLM patterns in existence. "This is not a resource problem. It is a design problem." "This is not a story about insufficient resources. It is a story about structural mismatch." The binary framing and the short second sentence are the tell. A human magazine writer occasionally uses this construction for deliberate effect. LLM text uses it as a default paragraph-ending rhythm. The fix is to collapse the two sentences into one nuanced statement or rewrite the claim entirely. "The problem is structural, not financial" is less theatrical and more durable.

Short declarative sentences clustered at paragraph endings are a related pattern. The essay closes several paragraphs with a single sentence designed to land as a revelation. Some of these earn their brevity. The final line earns it. Others are manufactured rhetorical moments that signal effort rather than insight. Read each short closing sentence and ask whether it is doing work or performing work. The ones that are performing should be absorbed into the preceding sentence.

Vocabulary audit: the word "structurally" appears throughout and is a significant marker. "Operational" used in non-technical contexts, "precisely" used for emphasis, "notably" and "fundamentally" as sentence modifiers, and "generates" where a human would write "produces" or "creates" — these are all worth flagging on a single-pass read. No word should appear more than twice in a 4,700-word essay unless the subject demands it.

Tricolon constructions — the "X, Y, and Z" parallel three-item lists — appear frequently and create a rhythmic regularity that reads as generated. Vary the pattern. Some lists can be two items. Some can be absorbed into prose without enumeration.

**Suggested Sequence for the Clean-Up Pass**

Work through the essay in five distinct passes rather than attempting to fix everything simultaneously. A single pass trying to catch all patterns at once produces inconsistency.

Pass one resolves every em-dash through restructuring. This is mechanical and can be done in one sitting.

Pass two identifies every "X is not Y. It is Z." binary and rewrites each as a single complex sentence.

Pass three reads only paragraph-ending sentences and tests each one for whether it earns its length or is performing.

Pass four runs a vocabulary audit on the specific words flagged above, plus any others that appear more than three times.

Pass five reads the full essay aloud at speaking pace. Any sentence that sounds like it is announcing its own importance rather than saying something should be rewritten. The read-aloud test catches rhythmic artificiality that silent reading misses.

**The Underlying Principle**

Mechanical replacement produces a different pattern, not an absent one. The goal is not to eliminate particular punctuation marks or words but to make the sentence structure varied and purposeful enough that no consistent algorithmic habit is detectable. Human writers are inconsistent in productive ways — they solve the same structural problem differently each time they encounter it. That inconsistency is what the clean-up passes need to introduce. The tool for producing it is editorial judgment applied sentence by sentence, not find-and-replace.

The essay has strong bones and several genuinely good sentences. The clean-up task is not a rewrite. It is a pass that makes it sound like one person wrote it across one long afternoon.
